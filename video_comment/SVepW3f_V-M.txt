Observations show that the breakdown voltage admits a minimum for a certain value p.d, it is therefore "easy" to trigger arcs. I know you are dumb on purpose Mehdi but pretty much all fuses are spring loaded in some way. Even the 220 V fuses, except the wire is also the spring. It's under tension even when it gets red hot. So should it melt, it "pings" off into whatever direction still has the most tension. That is how you can see it's broken as you have a small bead of molten wire with a decent gap between it and the other side. It doesn't just droop away and fall to the side of the tube.  Car fuses make that squiggly path as that path is the spring. It's thicker than the actual fuse part and so when it blows, the thin wire separates reliably because the thicker part is holding it under tension. Might even be some material science involved where the hotter the thick section gets, the more it pulls the thinner section apart.  And fuses that can be reset are essentially just material science, they are resettable because they don't actually break. They just get hotter and so they bend apart and they need to cool down again before being able to make contact, which they of course don't do automatically as that would be deadly. But should some genius fix the lever in place, they still just bend apart and once they cool they MIGHT (if bad design) make contact again. But they won't stay in contact if there is a short circuit.

@ElectroBOOM The breakdown voltage in any gas follows a Paschen curve. While extremely good vacuum (< 10^-3 Torr) greatly increases the breakdown voltage and prevents arcs, the breakdown voltage actually initially decreases compared to atmospheric pressure until it reaches a minimum around 1-10 Torr, which depending on your vacuum system could well be the regime you are in especially with non rotary vane pumps. The is because the ionised charge carriers smash into less atoms at lower voltages because there is less gas overall, meaning the charges can make it further distances and hence sustain the arc over longer distances. However at some point you start decreasing the number of atoms that can ionise and carry the arc and further reduction of the pressure reduces it even further, which is why the breakdown voltage eventually starts increasing again.   https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paschen%27s_law

In general, glass-sealed fuses, including those used in microwave ovens, are typically filled with an inert gas, such as nitrogen. The purpose of the gas is to provide a medium inside the fuse that does not support combustion and helps to prevent the formation of arcs when the fuse blows.  When a fuse blows, the metal inside the fuse melts or vaporizes, creating an open circuit. The presence of an inert gas helps in quickly extinguishing any potential arc that might occur when the circuit is interrupted. This is important for safety reasons, as it helps minimize the risk of fire or damage to surrounding components.  The specific gas used can vary between manufacturers and fuse types, but inert gases like nitrogen are commonly employed for this purpose. The gas is sealed within the glass or ceramic tube during the manufacturing process, creating a controlled environment that enhances the safety and reliability of the fuse.
