I'm going to have to disagree with almost everything in this video.  I use a decent UV filter for protection (no need to go crazy, $40 will get you a decent filter in a Kenko or Hoya) and very rarely have to even touch the front element of the lens. I have lenses that are years old that don't need a clean. The UV filter can be cleaned with water, 100% isopropyl alcohol (for oil and grease that won't shift) and common plain tissues then use a blower when they're dry if there is any lint left behind. Be very careful with isopropyl though as it can destroy certain coatings and types of plastic (which is again why I'm happy to put it on a $30 filter, not $1500 lens). If you think the filter has degraded try another and if that fixes things use the old filter as protection in storage only and buy a new one for shooting.  The front glass has to be filthy or quite badly hazed to destroy IQ - a fingerprint or two actually won't make as big a difference as you might think. A uniformly bad piece of glass (like a bad filter) will do much more damage. Scratches surprisingly will only affect an image noticeably if they happen to catch the light at the correct angle. Rear glass is a different story - a scratch on the rear element of the lens is a photo-ruining catastrophe.  Not much need to verify the sharpest apertures for a lens. WIde open is always going to be softer, f/8-f/11 is going to be the sweet spot depending on your lens, and stopping down much further than f/11 you'll see diffraction softening. That doesn't mean you should avoid wider or more stopped down apertures though. For example it may well be worth shooting at f/22 or even above if you're shooting a landscape or macro and want to sacrifice a little bit of sharpness for a lot of depth of field. If you're shooting in low light or shooting soft portraits wider apertures may well be something you want to sacrifice to freeze motion or get shallower depth of field. Shooting at the sharpness sweet spot is fine if you're shooting for forensics but limits any kind of artistic choice.  Shooting a ruler, I'll grant you isn't a bad way to set your focus fine tuning. But you can print a focus chart designed for this for the price of some ink and a piece of paper and that'll probably make your job a bit easier. I wouldn't actually buy a ruler specifically to do this. Also autofocus micro-adjustments on a zoom lens (like the 24-85mm you're using) can be problematical. Quite often you might get, for example,  -5 correction needed for 24mm end and say, +10 needed at 85mm end. Choosing either of these will seriously degrade the lens performance for the other focal length. Best options are, if the values are widely separated, either choose a value mid-point or, preferably check back on the lens usage and see which focal length is used the most an calibrate for that. Depth of Field at 85mm will be less so maybe better to adjust for that as this won't be such a problem at 24mm. If the values are too widely spaced and you're unhappy with sharpness only real option is to replace the lens. Sounds drastic but chances are that the camera and lens construction tolerances were at the extremes of quality control - both fine individually but when together they compound the problem.
