Those little bubbles on the crust are due to fermenting the dough in the fridge.  The process of placing a dough in a fridge/freezer is called 'retardation' and doesn't harm the dough too much, it does cause crystallisation of the natural sugars found in the flour, sucking moisture out of the dough and giving it a 'skin'.  This process does however aid in producing a thicker crust, which is a desirable trait in a good sourdough!

I wouldn't (completely) agree on that thing with that springing back...  There are basically four different types of proofing. You can find out by pressing with your finger around half a centimeter (around a fifth of an inch, don't press in to much).  If it springs back completely... this actually isn't good.... It means it hasn't proofed completely. The oven rise is quite decent but it might not have the perfect consistancy after baking, as well as flavour. Better proof it a little longer.  The next to states are both good, depending on what you want.  If the dough springs back rather quick but still leaves a little dent  - that's exactly what you want, if you are after a rustic, wild oven spring. This is because the dough will have the greatest oven rise. Flavour and consistancy are great, you are ready to rock. It rises like a maniac, the crust springs like crazy and the crum is great.  Number three: The dough springs back a little, but not to much and leaves a bigger dent. This means you have a bread, that has risen allmost all the way outside the oven and will rise only a little bit in the oven. You won't get a oven spring , or at least not a big one. This is great, too, if you want it to be like that. When you try to proof the dough this much the goal usually is to not get oven spring at all and get a nice smooth surface. as I said, a matter of taste. I like both, but it is actually way harder to not get a wild spring.. Other then the spring, the crum and taste is very compareable to the state before.  Aaannndd last... and certainly also least... number four. This is when you overproofed it. You press your finger in aaannddd.. Nothing happens. If it doesn't spring back at all... the dough is pretty much dead. No oven rise at all, no good results in basically anything.   This by the way works for almost all doughs, that are working with living organisms. So if you work with yeast it works the same way.   And there is nothing wrong with using yeast (but only use yeast OR sourdough, don't put to many organisms in there). There is basically ONE thing that makes a bread great or make suck: TIME.  Naturally, if you don't use yeast packages and just sourdough, it simply takes time, otherwise it won't work. Therefore you are adding the most important ingredient - time - naturally to the dough.  The thing with yeast is - people put in that shit like maniacs, to have fresh bread in very few hours. It might be fast, but not delicous. Time will make the flour soak in water and enzymes will develope the flavour. This sure get's lost when using much yeast.  But if you greatly decrease the yeast, but increase the time, you'll get brilliant results, too. Sourdough has the advantage, that bread usually stays fresh longer and it has a characteristic flavour, yeast breads are just different, but not neccesarely bad.  But if you do that... throw away that instant yeast packages.. They suck. I use like 5 to 10 grams of fresh yeast MAX for a bread. You are barely able to measure those small amounts with dried yeast , you need redicolous small portions of those stuff if you want to do it right. . I keep that shit just for emergencies, when I forgot to buy yeast.
