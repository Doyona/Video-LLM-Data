Magnets are a great trick for old plaster and lath walls.  Most (cheaper) stud finders will struggle with these.  Buy some small  neodymium magnets (Home Depot, etc.) for a few bucks.  Use them directly on the wall - sliding them lightly around the wall until you feel a pull.  There is a nail in that location.  (The magnet may even stick to the wall).  Scan above and below that location... if you find additional nails in a vertical line then 99% chance there is a stud there.  Move over 16 to 24 inches and scan again.  You will find a pattern of studs (hopefully) spaced at regular intervals.  Another trick, if the nails are buried deep, is to tie/tape the magnet to a string and slide it along the wall while it dangling by the string.  It will tend to "stick" to the wall in places where nails are.
This wouldn't work for someone who has plaster walls.

There's another thing that can help you get an idea of APPROXIMATELY where the studs are (in the absence of boxes or other visible clues) using your EARS.  No - not to hold the magnet.  Try rapping on the wall with a knuckle and listen to changes in TONE.  Between studs you have a more hollow sound, but as you approach a stud the tone tightens up to indicate the general area where you can start applying your magnet.  Now, on a typical residential 8' wall you should rap above or below the midline (4' above the floor) where there MAY be fire-blocks between the studs, possibly required by some (more anal) building code police.  Cheap, quick, non-destructive, yes; foolproof, painless, no.  Good hint, though, the magnet thing. Afterwards, hold a taunt plumb line or just a chalk sign near the ceiling and against the wall --or as high up as the wall space you are working on.  Move it horizontally across the located nails within one vertical line.  Eyeball the nail marks in relation to the hanging line so that locations are djstributed between the line.  Because across the wall every 4 feet, (usually) two pieces of wall board are butted up against each other at the center of the stud.  These will have nails placed alternately on either side of the center of the stud.  Likely, the center can be found fairly accurately  using a plumb line/chalk line, as was just mentioned. If you are slow enough with the swing, and if the magnet is brushing against the wall, it should be attracted to the studs.

In regards to ceilings, here in Massachusetts the builders have a useful trick of nailing furring strips - 1x2's- to the underside of the joists.  They nail these perpendicular to the joists for strength.  Once the drywall is screwed to the furring strips but before the insulation fills the bays it makes the electrician's job super easy slipping wires under the joists.  It also allows the electricians to maintain the joist's integrity by not having to drill wire egress-holes through load-bearing joists.  The net result of this, though, is that the drywall screws now run at right angles to the joists.  You may never know this until you cut a hole for a light can into the middle of what you think is an empty bay - and run right into the stud 3/4 inch above the drywall!
