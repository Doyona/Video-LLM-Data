From Failure to Fantastic!  I made about 8 batches within the last two weeks (when I first started making these). Here is what I learned.  1) Do not use a whole packet of yeast. The whole packet is way too much yeast. You need to use only 1/4 tsp of the packet. The whole packet is 2 & 1/4 tsp of yeast. I did this on accident and my bread was super bland.  2) Weight measure your flour! (320g) This part is important, especially if you don't aerate your flour. I was just cup measuring straight from the bag. My buns came out, decent, but maybe too dense. Once I started weight measuring, these came out perfect. Fluffy deliciousness that was not overly dense. Perfect. I had a kitchen scale the whole time, why didn't I think of this sooner!  Also, I have typically been using the overnight method vs the 3 hour method. The practice of using hot tap water for tea or in recipes is problematic. Some sources claim it's Ok; others say it's a health risk.  Consider this then.  Most, if not all, water heaters incorporate a sacrificial anode made of one of the following metals - magnesium, aluminum, or zinc.  Without going into details they slow the corrosion of the iron tank by their own corrosion.  Ergo, you have either magnesium, aluminum, or zinc salts in your hot water which may or may not be unhealthy but more than likely will affect the taste of the bread.  Since most households have a microwave oven it makes more sense to heat the water in the oven.  A cup of cold tap water in a 1000 watt oven will be uncomfortably hot in about 50 secs.  One can always use the wrist test to check temp; however, since instant read thermometers are cheap (about $5) I'd recommend purchasing one for general use around the kitchen.  Whenever I make yeast breads I always use a digital thermometer regardless of the liquid of choice.  Plus I proof my yeast before adding it to the flour but only because that's how I've always done it.  It's a step you can skip as long as your are not using yeast past it's expiration.  I proof because it gives the yeast a nice head start and only takes 10 minutes.
