In the problem we don't have some critical information - how many people are in each group, which would be relevant if we have that 3rd possibility where rash didn't got better or worse, i.e. stayed roughly the same. In this case each group would be divided into 3 subgroups by result - got worse, didn't change, got better. And results shown to us show only 2 of these 3 groups, which means we have no information how many people didn't have changes in their condition.  Here's an example how that would change things: Let's assume that both groups have the same number of people(we have no evidence for that, it's just an example): N = 223+75+X = 107+21+Y or we can simplify: Y = X + 223 + 75 - (107+21) = X + 170; Where X is the number of people that didn't have changes while using cream and Y - number of people that didn't have changes while not using cream. And N is the number of all people in a group which is equal to both groups. Now we'll have to split question "Did the skin cream make the rash better, or did it make worse" into two questions: 1) "Did the skin cream make the rash better?" 2) "Did the skin cream make the rash worse?" And now we can use proportions to answer both questions. Question number 1 can be answered by comparing 2 numbers. First number is - how many people got better? Second number is - how many people DIDN'T get better? For the group that used cream those numbers are 223(i.e. got better) and 75+X(i.e. got worse plus didn't change) For the group that didn't use the cream those numbers are 107(got better) and 21+Y(got worse plus didn't change) We know that Y = X+170. Let's substitute Y. so for the group that didn't use cream numbers are: 107(better) and 21+X+170=X+191 So proportionally speaking 223 over 75+X is bigger than 107 over X+191. That's also obvious from the fact that we assumed that both groups have the same number of people and 223 is a bigger fraction from the same number than 107: 223/N > 107/N (because in our case N is always positive) So the answer to the first question "Did the skin cream make the rash better?" is Yes! But it DOESN'T mean that answer to the second question is No. We can check it by looking at numbers for the second question we have: For the group that used cream 75 is how many people got worse, and 223+X is how many people didn't get worse And for the group that didn't use the cream - 21 is how many people got worse and 107+Y is how many people didn't get worse. Similarly 75/N is bigger than 21/N, so the answer for the question "Did the skin cream make the rash worse?" is also Yes! In our example skin cream accelerates both people getting better and getting worse. And in control group that didn't use cream more people kept their state unchanged. So the cream both made the rash better and worse at the same time! This video is the danger of putting too much stock in behavioral research. Behavioral experiment design is often lazy, and usually flawed even when it isn't lazy due to the sheer difficulty of capturing such a complex emergent phenomenon.  It's important to note that, simply in the interpretation of statistical data, the question is VERY materially different when asking about a controlled medical trial of a skin cream to treat rashes vs when it is about a diff-in-diff test on the effect of a policy on a tertiary statistic with no specific details offered.  When you ask them about the skin cream, they imagine a controlled medical trial, where the experiment design is such that other possible impacts are minimized.  The cream is (ostensibly) intended to directly cause one of the two possible outcomes. The outcome is a simple and single observable event in each observation. The 'improvement' of a rash is something which is easily understood...it is less visible and/or causes less discomfort. There is no room for interpretation here. When you ask them about the gun laws, none of that is true. The person imagines a very different data generating process. There is no controlled experiment, so the cities are different and in different places, have different cultures, demographics, and histories and different events between the passing of the law and the observations, etc. Also, the laws are likely different in important ways, and so less perfectly comparable. Not only that, but the legal status of gun in the various cities probably varied quite a lot BEFORE the laws were passed, anyway. The law is, at best, intended to INdirectly cause one of the two possible outcomes, with multitudinous omitted variables that active political participants (particularly the more numerate ones) are used to thinking about frequently. And finally, the outcome observed is a simple count of whether or not crime "improved" in each city, with no context, specificity, or detail concerning what the hell that actually means. Were there fewer crimes committed? What kinds of crimes are counted in the analysis? Did the other kinds of crimes also decrease? Was it a reduction in crime severity? Maybe it caused fewer crimes to be reported? What about arrest/conviction rates? You get my point.  The problem is not that numerate people are more polarized. It's that numerate people recognize that the "correct" answer to this question is meaningless and intellectually dishonest without additional context. The asker refuses to provide the necessary contextual information, and so the numerate person inserts their preferred context, which will force the interpretation that the grossly over-simplified (but intended) conclusion which opposes their political position is in fact unreliable. You see it in the video interviews here...THIS claim of unreliability is the 'wrong' answer. They don't misread the numbers. They don't get the answer wrong. They turn the unanswerable question into an answerable one by adding context. It's the fault of the researcher for not recognizing the importance of that context.
