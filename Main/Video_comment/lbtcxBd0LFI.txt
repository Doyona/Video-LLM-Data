That kind of screwdriver from flat bars is how we learn to do it at the Liege's gunsmithing school. It allow us to easily cut a screwdriver for the exact length of the screw. When working on luxury handmade shotguns you need to have a screwdriver the exact same width, depth and length as the screw slot to not damage it, and we fancy extremely thin slots (we use metal sawblade that we ground flat and thin to make them) that let the engraver a lot of space.  Each screw is custom made the slot cut parallel or perpendicular to the gun. After adjusting the action and mating of the bore, we mark where the slot will be, cut the head to length, slot them using the marks and file the head flush with the body with a now very thin and shallow slot that needs a custom screwdriver to not ruin. Each gunsmith use their own slotting tool and matching screwdrivers so we don't need to cut a screwdriver every time. Although if we are repairing or servicing a gun we might need to make screwdrivers for it.  The technique to use them is to put your work on the bench on a cloth so it doesn't scratch and mar the finish and also doesn't slip. Then you place the screwdriver in the slot and place the handle right under your shoulder and collarbone while holding the handle with a full grip. The pressure on the screwdriver should come from the shoulder and the twist from your hand. To twist the screwdriver you should move your whole body to keep your grip strong.  That way you can apply maximum and uniform pressure and twist with a big lever (as you can lock your wrist and use your body). That's how you tame those ridiculously thin and shallow slots which doesn't use any anti-seize (only coating exists to us, Ballistol for the metal and linseed oil for the wood, period).   Luxury/handmade side-by-side shotguns are made using the 1900s tech.

I would avoid the excessive hammering on the heat treat wrap. You need to have the package air tight, but often beating them shut like leads to hole in the seam or the corners. Generally the place I work we just double fold the edges, and lightly tap them for best results.  Also, to help with the warping many steels will have a normalizing temp, then a heat treatment temp.  Ideally you take the part to the normalizing temp and let it, well normalize, or fully thru heat to that temp. After that you ramp it up to the heat treatment temp and let it cook. This decreases the temperature differential and thus expansion differential between the out side and the inside of the part and decreases stress it creates so less warping. Unfortunately, this dose tend to about double the amount of time your parts take in the oven which is why it often gets ignored.

So fun bit of trivia, the reason why hollow ground screwdrivers are sometimes done in this flat plate style is because it makes dressing and reshaping the tips much simpler in the end-user's shop.  It provides a pair of dead flat faces, so in similar fashion to how you fabricated these, the end user could clamp them down and reshape the tip without losing parallelism.  This was especially critical in the days before mass manufacture of fasteners, as each one was essentially custom sized, so if you were a gunsmith tasked with fixing up whatever finely finished hand crafted double rifle wandered into your shop by way of London or Belgium or what have you, you'd often find yourself needing to grind custom sized screwdrivers to not risk marring the finish.  Nowadays it's mainly retained out of tradition, but it's still nice to be able to custom fit tools to the job.

It's been so long that I forget the details, but those gunsmithing drivers have the flat shank apparently for torque/torsion reasons. A lot of screws in guns are delicate relative to the force they have to produce and withstand, so there's not a lot of room for error. They're high-precision, low-threshold. If I remember correctly, the screws are usually of a similar metal as the hole to resist corrosion, so there's no "give" (I forget the term at the moment) in the screws. The flat shank drivers allow you to feel when they're at the right torque, as the shank will flex in a more noticeable way than other shank styles.   Anyway, Awesome video so far!
