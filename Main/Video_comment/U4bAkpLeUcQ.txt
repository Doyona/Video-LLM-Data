In the broader picture, our national fascination with rail trails (most of which are much more poorly executed, of course, because they lack the high-profile support this one got) serves mainly to pit pedestrians and cyclists against rail passengers, closing the door to potential future rail services since you would now have to take "parkland" (such that it is) away from people in order to run trains. North America call them "ties". It is an old shipbuilding term carried over to the railroad(railway). Long timbers were fixed/tied between the Bow & Stern frames of a ship during building to maintain its shape/gauge, as do todays "ties" on the railroad tie together two rails.  In Britain we call them "sleepers". To move anything across the ground with ease, lengths of timber were placed on the ground as a roadway and items could them be dragged across them even carts and wagons. These wooden roadways were called "Sleeps" and hence the wooden lengths were called "Sleepers". And gradually these sleeps would have rails/plates fitted to them to improve the way to guide the carts and wagons and so it stuck.   The French call them "traverses" ("traverses de chemin de fer"). The Germans "Schwellen"; the Spanish "traviesas" or "durmintes"; the Itaians "traverse" or "dormiglioni".; the Swedes "slipers"; the Nowergians "sviller"; the Danes "sveller"; the Finnish "kiskopuu"; and my favourite, the Dutch "dwarsliggers". The original 1850s West Side Line ran on the streets, specifically on 10th and 11th Avenues, escorted by the West Side Cowboys, all the way down to St. John's Park (where the entrance to the Holland Tunnel now is). But there were too many pedestrian deaths over the decades, so in 1926 the City and the New York Central agreed to grade-separate the railroad as part of an improvement project led by Robert Moses. The High Line was completed in 1936, but with the advent of trucks, traffic declined and it was closed by 1980. In both incarnations, it served only freight clients, not passengers. BUT there was a Fantrip at some point, which is where the photos of passenger railcars comes from. The MTA did consider at a couple of points buying the line from CSX and using for passenger service, as light rail or an extension of the 7 line, but obviously that never came to fruition.
