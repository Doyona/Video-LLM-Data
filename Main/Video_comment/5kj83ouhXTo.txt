Hmm, nice.  :)  I think the real catch is going to involve the aluminum.  The catch with aluminum as a battery or even a battery-super-capacitor material is that not unlike carbon, Aluminum can form chemical triple-bonds.  On the one hand this means a richer-than average energy density, but unlike carbon, which only sometimes  forms triple-bonds, Aluminum is really aggressive  about forming strong triple-bonds with both itself and oxygen.  No really, you can make a _potentially_ excellent metal-air battery with aluminum, but you can't *recharge it, ever.*  The oxygen will always form triple bonds with the aluminum that the electrolyte can't break.  Sure you can compensate for this with enough boron in the cell (there's some research in France and the UK on this) but this reduces either specific energy or specific power considerably, just to get an aluminum based cell to recharge *at all.*  What I'm saying is, a graphene-aluminum system can work, but it would have to be sealed air-tight to keep O2 in the atmosphere from killing the battery dead.  OR you'd have to find a way to make a graphene-aluminum/aluminum-air hybrid that can still recharge?  Somehow?  That would be best of both worlds since the specific energy of the original Alcoa battery (aluminum-air) still beats Li-ion designs to this day, but the recharging is the thing.  Nobody's going to buy a battery with a cycle life of ONE for anything serious (more serious than backup for alarm clocks, smoke alarms, etc.).  There's no question though, an aluminum-ion cell, using graphene, sounds awesome as hell, provided you can keep the triple-bond chemistry from happening.

The battery chemistry voltage isn't really as huge of an issue as you might think.  It's pretty normal these days for sophisticated electronic devices like smartphones and laptops to have some form of DC-to-DC step up/down circuitry to take the 2.7-4.2 volts of a lithium ion cell and convert it into whatever voltage the device needs. Laptop computers, for instance, need several different voltages internally and so have to step things up as high as 12 volts to power devices on the PCI bus like graphics cards and storage drives and for thunderbolt/USB-PD or down to as low as 1.2 volts for the CPU.  Much simpler devices typically are designed to simply Just Work(tm) on the voltage range of a lithium cell or even on the 0.8 to 1.5 volts range of an alkaline cell (or the 0.8 to 1.2 volt range of a nickel-metal-hydride rechargeable cell) or a pair of such cells in series. You'd be surprised just how tolerant of operating voltage a lot of simple electronic devices are!  Hearing 1.7 volts for the prototype graphene-aluminum cell chemistry makes me think that it could just be used in series to get roughly the nominal voltage of a single lithium cell. Or alternatively, use a step up converter to boost it up to the nominal 3.6 volts of a lithium cell.  The big difference however is how these new batteries have to get charged. They're going to require their own charge circuitry that may be pretty similar or pretty different from whatever is used for NiMH or lithium batteries. NiMH charges using constant voltage whilst lithium ion charges through constant current. Which of those charging methods will graphene-aluminum batteries require? Will they require a special charge curve like lithium batteries do for maximum battery life? Or can you just whack 'em with a dumb flat curve like you can do with lead-acid chemistry? (For context, your 12 volt car battery gets recharged by a 14 volt constant voltage from the alternator.)  Those are going to be the interesting questions for a recreational/hobbyist electrical engineer like me to know the answers to. üëÄ
