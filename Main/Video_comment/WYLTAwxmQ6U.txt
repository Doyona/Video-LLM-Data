"The Sun kills vampires" thing wasn't a part of the vampire lore found in the earliest gothic novels from the 1800's which more or less spawned the concept of the modern vampire. It was a creation of Hollywood, to have a quick and dramatic way to kill a vampire. In fact, prior to those books, there really was no traditional of vampires as we know them,  they were simply malevolent undead spirits who might use blood to extend their lives, and even that wasn't consistent. That they were nocturnal has more to do with their feeding habits (it's easier to feed on humans when they are asleep than awake). In the earliest vampire stories (The Vampyre - 1819 (which incidentally was an expansion of the story that lost to Frankenstein), and Carmilla -1874), their primary way for feeding and creating offspring vampires was seduction. In 'The Vampyre', Aubrey travels with the vampire Lord Ruthven across Europe, presumably during the day, without noticing anything odd about his traveling arrangements.

But yes, let's entertain this idea for a moment.  Classic vampire weaknesses, from older lore, not teenage dramas... would be garlic, holy water, and crucifixes. They could be killed by a stake through the heart, setting them on fire, beheading, or exposure to direct sunlight. We obviously don't really know any instances in which Snape came into contact with almost any of these things, and one could definitely argue that garlic might've kept him out of Quirrell's classroom/office/sleeping quarters, but we do know that he's had run ins with both fire and direct sunlight, neither of which killed him. Granted, the fire could've killed him if he hadn't put it out, but it kills regular humans as well, so I don't think that's very vampire exclusive. The more obvious one is direct sunlight. Snape has been seen outside in the courtyard during school breaks, for example when he confiscated Harry's Quidditch Throughout the Ages book. That would've definitely placed him outside during the day, thus exposing him to direct sunlight. If he'd been a vampire he would've become a shiny ball of fire almost instantly. Yet he walked away unscathed. We also know that vampires are supposed to have regenerative qualities, healing wounds much faster than any human. If Snape had been a vampire from childhood on, he would've stayed at that age, physically, for the rest of his existance. Well, and then there's the last one... and that's the fact that he dies. Wolf packs are a prolonged falasey. This was misinterpreted by observation of what appeared to be dominant Alpha and Beta members leading small groups of unrelated wolves of various ages. In reality, wolves live in family groups centered around mating pairs. They also mature rapidly compared to our own growth cycle and can have successive litters of multiple pups. The Alpha and Beta have actually been confirmed to just be the mating pair and the other wolves in the "pack" are the offspring of staggered age. It does not nessisary impact the point about lupin as Rowling likely wrote his character under the same common wrong steriotpe of wolves. What is interesting is the point about dogs fighting for dominance while wolves don't. Under the pack steriotype this is actually incorrect. In a pack, the inference is that the Alpha is an interchangeable individual decided through competative acts that would be successively challenged and ultimately usurped in time. The true nature of wolves more aligns with what is described in this video as the individual would leave and start their own family group at full maturity and thus have no reason to challenge the leading parent for dominance. With dogs, this behavior is more artificially developed by us through a breakdown of that family based dynamic (we take an artifical Alpha role and often separate out dog families to fit our pet needs) and artifical interaction with outside groupings (family dynamics of dogs are often brought into direct interaction due to our own social structures.) In the wild you would rarely see wolf family groups interact but if they do it would likly be in a competative nature.
