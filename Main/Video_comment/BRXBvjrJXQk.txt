I really love that each intro for Critical Role has been unique in their own ways.  The original intro for campaign 1, the low budget cosplay intro. It felt like a bunch of kids that used their pocket money to be able to buy some props to cosplay as their PCs. The second intro for campaign 1, the high budget cosplay intro. It felt like a bunch of full time cosplayers that have the time, money and resources to be able to create cosplays of their PCs. The original intro for campaign 2, the funky 80s romp intro. It invoked the image of a bunch of friends in the 80s heading over to their friend's place to play D&D and have fun regardless of background. The second intro for campaign 2, the legendary animated intro. It really showed the viewers the world and who the PCs are, giving us hints of their backstories, their adventures and what they could face in the future. The updated second intro for campaign 2, just a more grander version of the animated intro. It added details to the already legendary intro. The Exandria Unlimited intro, shows off these beautiful landscapes and stained glass windows that depict locations and significant events in Exandria. The original intro for campaign 3, the explorers intro. It showed the players and DM exploring this temple, a stand in for the world, dressed as stereotypical 19th-early 20th century explorers' gear. And now the second intro for campaign 3, the rotoscoped intro. It blends the fantastical world with the animation with the real world players by animating over their actions.  All of them are unique in their own ways yet still feel distinctly Critical Role. Sure some will be more loved than others (everybody will absolutely love the animated intro and the updated animated intro), but you can't say they are bad. They all do their job and do them well. A lot of work would've had to go into each one, and we must accept that.  All that said, I look forward to see this intro more, though I do need to catch back up due to IRL obligations, and I look forward to what they end up making for the next intro, as given their track record, it will be unique and exciting. I watched all of campaign 2 and am doing my darnedest to catch up to campaign 3, which I'm enjoying greatly :) I'm a fan of what the show is doing and I'm sure the Critical Role team is working hard to keep it up  I'm about to rip into this intro, but I will say, other folks have pointed out there may be a sync issue, and its very possible that this is what tipped me over from "not everything can be a masterpiece and this just isn't to my tastes" to "this is bad and its disappointing that it even made it to release"  But I cannot say I enjoyed watching this. The jump cuts look like animation errors. Everyone's faces fall into the uncanny valley. The visual effects are a mess, that is, the paint splotches, glitter, distortion, etc., whoever was responsible for sticking this in After Effects was not doing their best work. The visuals are hard to parse and the stylistic, colourful water colour theme I expected is muddy and confusing. Also the rotoscoping just seems poorly done, like it was either done by AI or an unskilled grunt as opposed to an experienced 2d animator. Facial features float around the form of the head and shapes jitter noticeably. In fairness, maybe it was also made difficult by the source live-action footage, I do not know.   And there's just not enough to redeem it. The water colour concept could create an exciting, joyful mood, but as I said, its just too muddy. Blending the cast with their characters is fun, but it fell flat. (i.e. Taliesin's hammer swing is sick but I paid more attention to his rigid angry face; Marisha seems to be missing her fair share of creepy effects, etc.). A well-executed, crisp, minimalist animation might justify the absence of lovingly-made backgrounds and/or easter eggs, but this isn't the case. Similarly, I could have looked past the messy rotoscoping and appreciated the novelty if there was more quality in the overall composition, but alas.  Idk, I'm not trying to be a dick. But even as an amateur artist I can see a few things went wrong along the way here. Hopefully things come together better next time around. I'd personally prefer a different intro than this for the time being, but I suppose its not a big deal regardless.

Rotoscoping at a low FPS is often worse to the human eye than both live footage and hand drawn animation. This looks like it was done at 12 FPS. Pure rotoscoping like this also takes away many animation principles, such as easing, anticipation, and over-exaggerating. As a result, everything will look really stiff, unless you try to make things look hyper realistic, which requires at least 24 or 30 fps.  3.) Choppy camera movements. The footage they rotoscope is footage with a moving camera. In animation, you can get by with animating at a lower frame rate, but often you still want to keep the camera movements at a high frame rate, otherwise it will make everything look choppy. Think of old school video games on the Nintendo: the characters animations are slow, but the background scrolls at 60 fps, making everything very smooth. Because CR provided footage with a moving camera, the people rotoscoping couldnâ€™t add in their own camera movements, and so the final animation ends up being much more choppy.
