It pains me to watch people making a wooden handle when they have no clue with regard to grain direction. At 27:19 you can see that Adam has got it completely wrong. For maximum resistance to breaking, face grain must be aligned with the wider left and right sides of the handle, with the edge grain along the narrower front and back edges. When viewed from the top hole of the hammer, the end grain should be pointing in the direction of the swing. On wooden baseball bats, the label is always imprinted on the face grain, and it was widely known that hitting the ball with the label side could easily break the bat. The optimum alignment of grain in hammer and axe handles is either forgotten knowledge or simply ignored these days.

@9:00 metallurgist here, another way to describe how annealing works is to talk about the grain structure of a metal. Metals have grain patterns just like wood but they can vary in size and shape, when a metal is hardened its grain structure looks like a pile of needles (called martensite) and this structure is very brittle. Essantally what annealing is doing is heating up the metals grain structure loosening the molecules and letting the metal cool slowly will change the grain structure to look more blobby (called austenite), hence why hardened tap will snap while a soft wood nail will bend when hit with a hammer.

Sorry in advance for a very pedantic comment (which I'm sure Adam already knows, but which might be useful or interesting to viewers).  At 8:25 Adam says "heat *is* the molecules moving faster" but that's actually temperature/thermal energy, not heat. He's using the terms interchangeably because in the ordinary day-to-day world they largely are interchangeable, but in science "heat" is the transfer of thermal energy, where "temperature" is the average thermal energy.  The relationship of the heat and the wiggling is sort of the relationship between force and velocity, I suppose. You're imparting a force (the heating) which causes an acceleration and ultimately results in a higher velocity (thermal energy/temperature).  To give a car example, you've basically said "hitting the accelerator pedal is speed". The increase in speed is causally related to the accelerator pedal being pressed but they're not exactly the same thing.

Heat treating hardened steel affects the grain size. The way to think about hardness from a metallurgical viewpoint is by thinking about how the atoms align themselves in the crystalline structure of the metal. The crystals form into grains of differing sizes the larger the grain size, the softer the metal. It's the smaller grains which lead to hard / brittle metals. Larger grain size leads to softer more ductile metals.

When you heat a piece metal, say a billet of steel or aluminum, the atoms that make up the metal begin to become "excited" and move faster. They have higher thermal energy, or heat. This faster movement expands the metal ever so slightly per degree centigrade. This is why large buildings and bridges can grow and shrink a few inches depending on the temperature. When the atoms are excited, they can slightly move out of the lattice structure they have aligned themselves in and other smaller atoms, such as carbon, can and will move into the hot metal. The process of annealing slowly cools the metal, allowing the majority of the trapped gasses out of the metal. However, when the metal is cooled quickly, such as with oil or even water, the atoms, namely carbon, become trapped in the metal. The atoms are few in number, but they fill in the lattice structure of the metal, making it more difficult for atoms to slide past one another, therefore making the material harder.   The same thing can be done with other metals, in the process known as alloying. Metals such as zinc and copper can be melted together to form a "new" metal with unique properties. Brass, made out of zinc and copper, has different properties than its component metals did. Alloys, depending on their atomic structure and the metals contained, can vary drastically in hardness.
Hi Adam! Materials scientist here. The question here (7:33) is why hardened steel softens with red hot  heat.  The main phenomenon contributing to the change of mechanical properties is the change of atoms  arrangement(martensite and/or austenite turn into ferrite and pearlite).  Reduction of the number of dislocations and grain growth play a minor role in temperatures around 930 F but are important at higher temperatures (above 1500 F).   Most metals and alloys are made of grains. Each grain is a single crystal.  There are several types of crystals. Each one has its own way to deform, by the slipping of planes of atoms.   Dislocations are defects that disturb the slipping of planes of atoms, enhancing hardness.

There are three factors that all interact to give you an alloy's physical properties.  The first and most obvious is the composition.  The next two are the deformation (shear) history and the thermal history (heat treatment). The properties of an alloy depend largely on the microstructure, overlaid on the basic properties of the metals. If you take an alloy and let it cool down from melt slowly enough you get a close enough to equilibrium lowest energy microstructure.  That's usually awful - large grains, uniform and ductile, soft. When you deform the metal, you break up that large grain size - or stop it from forming as it cools.  When you heat treat it, quench it, you don't give it time to form that ideal grain and phase it would eventually form, but gets stuck in an intermediate state - which can be a completely different phase structure.   for example, when you quench carbon steel, you form Martensite is iron crystals with carbon atoms stuck between the iron atoms rather than forming a distinct separate phase like graphite or iron carbide - those stuck carbon atoms make the iron lattice stressed wherever there is a Carbon atom and that stops the mechanism that allows deformation from moving easily - so the yield point is dramatically increased so much so that the iron becomes very hard and brittle. When you then temper it, you allow some of the carbon atoms to move to a more ordered configuration, relieving the stress and softening the steel...  Tempering is an annealing treatment.  You give the metal a chance to move towards that ideal microstructure.  The other part of making metal is the deformation treatment.  That results in texture and deformation miocrostructures that you can't get by heat treatment alone - not even close!  So you can anneal hardened steel but if it's properties included specific deformation processing, annealing will destroy that too and you can't get that back by heat treating.

As little as I know about metallurgy... First, heating something, cooling something... that hardening thing goes about steel. Other materials may or may not behave in a similar way, most of them do not. The chocolate molecular structure is nothing like steel. But accidentally, it can be thermally treated in some similar way. Second, hardening of steel is about iron and carbon atoms arranging themselves into different structures. These are all crystal structures, nothing amorphous here. But different crystal structures have different properties and do exists at different temperatures and pressures. It so happens, that when you heat and then cool steel, at some point it goes into the hard and brittle structure, and if you cool it "instantly" with water or oil, from right temperature, it gets "stuck" in a hard phase. If you cool it down slowly, it passes the hard phase goes to the soft one. Quite convenient for working with it. And all that goes about steel. The mix of iron, carbon and some other stuff, all in the right proportions. No carbon, no hardening. The nail (common, old school) is usually iron. Won't harden. The file is a just right amount of iron and carbon, hardens very well. Sharp, but rather brittle. For hammer it is somewhere in between. More carbon than a nail, less than file. So in general, whatever you want to build, the steel needed is different. You can make something out of random chink of steel, but it may or may not work very well. And if you want to use the hammer on anything harder than chalk, well... you probably would like to harden it. The steel used in this project has being hardened and used to be a part of some construction thing... so it is probably close enough. I saw a few pointers here in the comments that you could benefit and learn from. Your handle leaves a bit to be desired. That abrupt edge at the end of the handle will cause you grief later on if you use the hammer a lot.  Also (and not to put too fine a point on this...) you did not heat treat to harden the hammer heads. That will cause problems if you use the hammer for metal working. You left the center-hole on the small head from when you were milling. It will cause raised dimples in metals that will be hard to eliminate and remove.  Also, as one of the viewers pointed out, you should have tapered the hammer head hole on both ends. A proper taper will create a waist in the middle of the hole. When you fitted the handle it's actually best to keep excess wood for the hammer head insert. The insert end should have been notch cut with a narrow slit. The leftover hardwood from when you cut the hammerhead insert, (I believe it's called a split tenon)... the leftover shoulder pieces could have been used as a wooden wedge insert into the split tenon. Then, the metal wedge could be driven in at an angle to provide even expansion of wood fibers that grips the handle and locks it into the hammer head. Tapering provides a more secure way to anchor the hammerhead to the handle. The 90¬∞ hole you milled will eventually begin slipping and will only get worse. The primary hammerhead should have been angled downward by about 1¬∞ for more controlled striking.  Because you did not heat treat your hammer head, it will always be prone to deformation. Excessive use on hard surfaces and a consistent blow on any one part of the hammerhead will create work hardening that could become prone to chipping. You have a fair understanding of machining, but it is lacking in key areas. When you cut the bar steel you should have oiled it.  You have 2 knife grinder sanding machines in your shop. You are using one for woodworking. But you could easily have fit metal sanding belts to the machine to grind it to a much finer finish than what you ended up with. I would have started out with 120 grit and finished with 2000 grit. Afterward it would easily have been polished up to a bright mirror finish far brighter than what you achieved. Your finishing was OK... and you ended up with a functional piece, but one that is prone to handle slippage because of the straight hole you milled into the head and handle.   You used a simple metal wedge and didn't split the tenon to receive a wooden wedge. The wooden wedge expands the split tenon to provide better and more consistent expansion of the wood into the hammerhead... Mr. Savage, it was a good effort, but definitely needs more work and improvement on your build technique. You need to "bone-up" on your metallurgy a bit more. It's an OK build, but could be so much better. I'd suggest taking some blacksmithing courses to learn how to punch hammer eyes and the proper way to fit handles to hammers, axes and hatchets.   Smithies who makes those kinds of tools will always deliberately leave the tenon longer than needed so they can fit the handle to the head properly. Any excess wood is trimmed off before the final steel wedge is driven in. They do this to cut and trim the hammer tenon flush with the hammer head. It also prevents the problems that you experienced while trying to set the handle to your hammerhead. You kept having to stop and reset the head to the handle. This does not occur with a properly tapered hammerhead eye, and a properly fitted handle.  This video link shows a smithy explaining and demonstrating how to fit hammer handles properly. It's different from what I know, but is an excellent demo for correct and proper hammer or ax/hatchet handle fitting. When you watch pay close attention to how much smaller the hammer handle tenon is from the hammer-eye. But notice how perfectly fitted the handle is when it's properly installed. Ideally, you want to make a flat with a mill bit first, then a small pilot hole with a pilot drill, then a small hole all the way through and then drill it to size. Let the drill do the work instead of forcing it through. This will introduce a lot of heat into the cutting surface of the drill, weakening it and making it wear much faster. Slower drilling means less heat and that means your drills last longer. A pilot hole helps with this as well as you're not cutting as much material at one nor are you using the point on a large drill, which is very inefficient.
