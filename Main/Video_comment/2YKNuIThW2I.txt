For repairing electronic stuff like this, retrace what likely happened. If reversed polarity power was applied, look at where that power comes into the PCB. Assuming you don't have schematics, physically trace the leads to whatever is downstream of those inputs and test stuff out, or if stuff is obviously damaged, just replace it. Often there will be diodes on these circuits that effectively act as fuses for this exact type of thing...diodes and resistors will be the most likely blown stuff. Transistors (which effectively contain diodes) are sensitive to this kind of thing too. IC's as well....these can often be harder to swap out, especially on SMD (non thru-hole) PCB's. Worst case scenario, bite the bullet and take the thing to an electronics repair shop. If you find someone good, this should be a pretty easy repair for them, even without schematics....it's a relatively simple circuit. Also, you may find you can get it partially working (ie. turns on, lights up, gauges mostly work) but once it's all back together in actual operation, things are off.....maybe a gauge doesn't read correctly, or behaves oddly....maybe there are issues downstream with other components which interface with the dash cluster. Often it's tough to tell the extent of the damage and how far the electricity got into the circuit before it was effectively choked off by a failing component. If you don't do a deep dive and get everything, you may end up having to come back to it and re-diagnose.....which isn't a huge  problem, just something to be aware of. Cool project, good luck!

One word of advice on repairing the cluster (from experience, having fixed VW clusters from the the previous generation and lots of electronics from that era): If you find any chips labeled "ULN2003" in there, just change them out. The ULN2003 is just a set of transistors for controlling things such as light bulbs, relays and the stepper motors moving the gauges in a cluster, but, in my experience, is quite fragile, mainly due to the complete lack of internal protection circuits, and is ALWAYS is the first thing to go up in smoke when a battery is hooked up in reverse. Even if they work fine at first, they will certainly fail down the road, so just change them out or have someone replace them for you if you have trouble soldering that size of chip. They only cost like half a pound, so definitely worth it when the alternative is having your cluster go out on the highway.
