Here are the 2 factors that are salient to frequent fliers, especially business travelers: where they live...  1) Which airport is easiet for them to depart from and   2) Where their destination is.   The reason that is salient is that different airlines have the majority of the routes to a particular market and some share. Those of us who worked in the industry for a decade or more know which airlines have the majority of the flights to a particular market which routes, and and which ones share that market.   If someone lives in/near the DFW metroplex, they're likely flying American no matter where they go because DFW is not only their HQ (decades ago it was NYC) plus their major hub of the hub and spoke system they operate.   If someone lives in/near Atlanta, chances are, they're flying Delta because that is their HQ and they own the vast majority of the routes into and out of ATL.   If someone lives in Chicago, they're likely choosing United because although it is the 2nd major hub in the hub and spoke system American uses to move it's aircraft around the globe, ORD is United's HQ and they still have the majority of flights in that market (with American being 2nd), unless that person's destination is ATL or DFW. Then, it might make more sense to fly DL or AA.   Similar considerations apply to flying internationally, but it's a bit more complex with people being more comfortable flying their "own" countries airlines unless there is an extremely reasonable fare that saves them hundreds of dollars and still departs from the most convenient airport from their city of origin and actually has flights to their destination.   The city of origin is first. The final destination is second. Then price, and all the other stuff factors in after those first 2 considerations. Business travelers tend to value comfort over leisure travelers who are actually paying for their flights and not expensing them at the end of the month.   Now, if there were an inch to 4 inches difference in "knee" room, that would be a possible game changer for people 5'9" and over, and especially those 6' 2" and over.   This is not only common sense, there are decades of data behind it, which is why the airlines began to squish more seats on the aircraft knowing most of their travelers are under 6' 2" tall. Trust me when I say this. They already know who will, and who will not, pay for the extra leg room in premium economy and business class, and they know how long the flight has to be for them to pay the extra amount.   When something significant impacts the airline's bottom line, only then will they *think* of making changes that require significant capital investments, like reconfiguring aircraft and taking an entire row of seats off a certain model.
