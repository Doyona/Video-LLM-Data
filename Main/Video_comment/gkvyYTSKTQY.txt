These are all very salient points. People don't realize just how much work can go into difficulty settings sometimes. A perfect example is Cyberpunk2077, and probably a contributing factor to why it was such a hot mess on launch, the devs simply didn't have the time they needed. When you change the difficulty in that game, it doesn't just juggle some numbers around. Pay attention to enemy behavior when you change the difficulty. On lower difficulty, they're less likely to take cover, quickhack attempts are few and far between, and their propensity for magdumping and then being trapped in a reload while you close the distance is way higher. On higher difficulties, they will use grenades to flush you out of cover, they will use flanking maneuvers, they'll hack you any time a camera or enemy can get line of sight, and they'll use every bit of cyberwear they have access to.  That means CDPR had to program a unique AI routine for each of the four difficulty levels. They had to program and balance each dynamic enemy four times over. That's a lot of work.

I feel like the difficulty issue was solved in the first Mario game for the NES, Super Mario Bros.  You had 32 stages across 8 worlds. As you progressed through the game, it would increase in difficulty and complexity, both in level layout and enemy placement. New players would likely not be able to complete the game in their first sitting. But the problem with catering to new players is that they don't stay new. A player playing through SMB1, and can only get to say.. 4-4, will eventually make it to 5-1. Someone who gets stuck on 8-2 will eventually get past 8-2. That was how older game and level designed worked, and it wasn't a bad system.  Now newer games (not just Mario games) pretty much make the difficulty of that of 1-1 through 3-4 for 90% of the game. And the rest of the game is only 10% of the remaining game but the rest of the difficulty we are missing, and frequently due to software and developmental limitations, gets cut off. So we never see the 8-3 or 8-4 type levels in some cases. And definitely not in the same percentage of content like we had before.  I'd prefer the same difficulty curve and same ratio of advancing in difficulty level. Not only does it provide experienced players a challenge, it ensures there is enough of the game to be satisfying is challenging. As well as giving not so experienced players more of the game to try and improve upon. Its interesting that this video touts Super Mario Wonder, but this was around the time of the release of the game. It hadn't been fully played by the community. While the game has a ton of charm and character that makes it a true Mario experience. It lacks the challenging content in the ratio I mentioned and even casual and less experienced players are being left thinking they wanted more of a challenge. There is challenging stages to be sure, but they are such a small percentage of the game that the amount of time playing it feels lacking.  The other reason to spread out the difficulty more evenly is replayability. The biggest example of this is Super Mario Bros. 3. The game itself can be 100% completed for the average person (for someone of sufficient skill to complete each stage) in about 3-4 hours. However the amount of time someone will spend playing SMB3 is far more than a few hours. You can easily get hundreds of hours. The game is a fun challenge every time you play it. Some of the newer Mario games don't exactly have that same level of replayability. I've wanted to make a video like this but using old school Sonic the Hedgehog as my example.  In the old Sonic games, you weren't ever suppose to full "lose" the game. Winning wasn't that hard, because you would only really die if you lost all your rings. When you got hit, they'd spill out, but you were relatively safe as long as you could grab just 1. The real game of Sonic, was to go fast.  The Sonic team had excellent level design to facilitate this. Generally, levels were divided into three paths, with the upper path being the fastest and the lowest path being the slowest. In order to stay on the upper path, you'd have to make daring jumps, bounce on precise places, move through platforms and make tight squeezes, and do it all without missing a beat. If you ever failed, you'd likely fall down onto the next path below. The lower paths would have more twists and turns. They'd have more mechanics that required you to stop to activate switches, pull levers, charge up spindashes, or clear enemies before progressing. The execution required to move forward on these paths was lower, but more time required. If you fell to the lowest paths, these would be incredibly slow. You might have to stand on a moving platform and wait for it to cross a gap. You might have to swim through water or oil which was slow and cumbersome. There were usually less enemies and instant death traps, but you couldn't really move quickly without having to stop for some forced platforming with wide margins of error but no room for improvement.  Therefore, two people could play the same exact game but have wildly different difficulties without changing any settings. A new player would often find themselves getting hit and falling down into the lower sections of levels. Taking their time and plotting forward. Meanwhile, seasoned players would be zipping over most of the course, using power-ups in specific ways to bound over obstacles and land amazing gymnastic feats to clear levels in seconds. This also meant that you could take more dangerous paths on some levels, while playing it safe on others. A very customizable game experience that didn't need to be explicitly stated to the player or tutorialized. Just by playing the game you would learn how to make it more to your liking, sacrificing speed for comfort or ignoring the risks to race the clock. Last year we released our first game on Steam: a shoot 'em up / STG / shmup called Schildmaid MX. We didn't make a game in this genre because 2D shooting games are easy to make (making a good one actually isn't easy at all and the best shmups are complex bits of design and programming work in a way that isn't always clear to observe by the average onlooker), but because we genuinely love these types of games.  The challenge was: a relatively successful shmup has an almost built in audience of hardcore players that are looking for the next game to challenge them... but we also wanted our game to avoid the brick wall difficulty that typifies the genre and be more welcoming to newcomers, while not betraying that audience.  So we decided to cut the content of our game, consisting of a large set of stages that gradually increase in diffiiculty, up in various game modes of varying challenge levels. So if you pick the easiest mode, it's not "baby difficulty" but its very own mode with its very own set of stages and other quirks and characteristics that make it a somewhat different experience from the other, harder game modes, which all also offer their own, individual experiences. This even makes the easiest mode interesting for veteran shmup players as it gives them the breathing room to get to grips with the very specific enemy bullet absorbing mechanics of our game and understand what it is exactly that we are asking from them and how it differs from the shmups they are used to.  Aditionally, save for one mode that is a 10 minute continuous, uninterrupted gauntlet, each mode has an optional harder route that is only accessible when the player meets certain, more challenging conditions. Then, layered on top of everything, we have incorporated an arcade game staple: a invisible difficulty slider that increases when the player makes progress without losing lives, to ensure constant pushback and friction, and drops just as fast when a player makes regular mistakes and loses lives along the way.  We like to think that we made a game that caters to a large variety of player skill levels and encourages and motivates players to keep pushing to get better and try the harder modes, without diminishing their accomplishments in the easier modes. Edit: I just remembered that you have a video where you correctly identify and describe feedback loops, so it was clearly just a misspeak here. Useful all the same for anyones whos interested  "negative feedback loop" actually means a restoring feedback loop, not a bad feedback loop. Rubberbanding or weapons to shoot the first place player in racing games are an example of negative feedback loop, because if you are doing poorly it pushes you toward being better, and if you are doing better it pushes you towards being worse. It is a restoring towards the mean.  A positive feedback loop is one that pushes you in the direction you are moving, an exaggerating force. Getting extra lives for hard challenges is actually a positive feedback loop, because the rich get richer. Likewise a racing game where there were shortcuts you couldnt make if you are going to slow is still a positive feedback loop, because being in last pushes you further behind.  I am from a medical background so these technical differences really matter when talking about things like hormones and the nervous system, but I think its useful for clariry in talking about game design as well.  To add to this, negative feedback loop is often called "balancing feedback", and positive is called "amplifying feedback" or "exacerbating feedback", and I think these are both more descriptive and precise. This was an enlightening, much-appreciated video. However, I think the point about one-off challenges in Super Mario Bros. 1 has to be placed in context. The game was designed as a 1-credit marathon run--it predates the save states and restore points it is often played with today, and even the now-obvious Continue Code wasn't explicitly pointed out in the game or its manual. This meant that, unlike in later games (Super Mario World on,) lives and coins really did matter. An important strategy was to seek out coins and 1ups early on, to last you through later stages where mistakes would inevitably accumulate and resources would inevitably be eaten through.  For instance, grabbing the hidden 1up you mentioned in 1-2 might technically be harder than ignoring it outright, but grabbing it _now_ is _much_ easier than facing Lakitu empty-handed later on, especially if you skip ahead via warp zone. I vividly remember mastering the early stages in order to accumulate as many lives as possible for Worlds 4 and beyond, and I'd always assumed Warp Zone was there for players confident enough to go without.  All of this also applies to the wealth of hidden coins, power-ups and 1ups, map-screen items, and other bonuses dug up in Super Mario Bros. 3, many of which are more hidden-object puzzle than platforming stunt. And this makes sense--Super Mario Bros. 3 is an even harder, longer game than the first, and the cost of warping from World 1 to 8 without stockpiling through the rest is even more dire. You could use a continue if you were desperate, but only up to a point--good luck beating World 8 in 3 lives!  In short, that particular bit of design is more a gamble than an unambiguous Red Pipe; picture, if you will, Bear Mario gathering mushrooms for the winter.  ...Or rather, it is in principle; the reason this system falls flat in later games like New Super Mario Bros. and Super Mario 3D World is that the introduction of save files and world-map backtracking rendered coins, 1ups, and bonus games redundant in practice--a whole economy of items designed for a game structure that had long since eroded away. By the time Odyssey dropped lives entirely, it had been a long time coming, since not doing so put a cap on the difficulty of individual levels (since, if you always have to start from scratch, the difficulty of completing the game becomes the product of the difficulties of each level!) This is why you'll often find easy-to-reach 1ups in difficult stages in Super Mario Sunshine, to simulate having infinite lives for particularly difficult (generally FLUDD-less) stages.

Congrats on turning 200!  Right around 22 minutes you mention something that's a **real** problem in video game design: Game rewards (particularly character leveling) intrinsically makes the game *easier* to play as you progress, inverting the desired difficulty curve. It's a huge problem in RPGs and strategy games, where the longer you play the more powerful items and skills and abilities you collect that make the game easier to play. This ends up requiring the game designers to implement artificial difficulty to try and keep the games balanced.   I play a lot of MechWarrior 5 and one of the best mods for the game (YAML) has introduced an "upgrades" feature. This feature locks you out of being able to drop heavier battlemechs until you buy the mech bay upgrades. You can't repair or refit certain items until you buy the repair bay upgrades. Other upgrades reduce the cost of repairs or reduce travel time. These upgrades end up having a positive feedback loop where the more upgrades you have the easier it is to play the game and get more upgrades until the game has become so easy that it's boring.  I love the mod and the upgrades feature, but it inverts the difficulty curve - making the game far more difficult to play for new players or new games, but so easy that it becomes banal for late game experienced players. (MW5 can be pretty flexible with this because of its open world difficulty zone design, and because mods can increase late game difficulty to improve late game balance.)  Dealing with that inverted difficulty curve requires very careful game design to ensure that each of those "upgrades" (or character levels or higher level items or spells or abilities) also opens up higher difficulty gameplay, while also keeping the escalating power levels from turning into an WoW-meme mess of stupendous nonsense.   This inverted difficulty curve problem is especially prominent in games (such as Oblivion or Skyrim) that deal with it by leveling opponents alongside the player. Leveling up doesn't feel very meaningful because all of the same enemies have also leveled up with you.   This inverted difficulty curve problem is worth its own video and I'd love to see you examine it in depth!
