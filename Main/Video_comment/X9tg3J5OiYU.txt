I think I might be able to answer who this book is _not_ for, but I could be wrong. It's not for anyone who likes roasts that are say 3-4 on a 1-5 roast scale (5 is obviously the exception because it's espresso). Which means that it's not for anyone with a "normal" definition of coffee, which means the vast majority of the coffee drinking population.  A funny thing about the light roast enthusiasts is that they're kind of like religious zealots, or coffee fashionistas is probably a better analogy. There's definitely something about this phenomenon that goes together with certain gentrified parts of the urban environment, where veganism, the lotus pose and organic smoothies are also on the menu. The lighter the roast the better, and paradoxically the less the drink actually resembles coffee. They call this "clean" (see the connection?)  I tried this with an open mind, I really did. I got fresh beans from an internationally lauded roaster (it cost an arm and a leg), brewed it to perfection, and found myself primarily looking for coffee notes. It was bizarre since I kind of wanted coffee, and also it wasn't particularly good. It wasn't bad, but it wasn't as good as an actual fruity drink, or actual coffee. So my title suggestion would be something like: "How to make an inferior fruity caffeine drink from coffee beans while removing as much of the coffee taste as possible in the process, and also espresso."
