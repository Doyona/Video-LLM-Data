By definition, something that's actually new, actually innovative, isn't affected by existing copyright precisely because it's NEW. It's separate from and unlike anything that has ever previously existed. Thus, no existing copyright could or does impinge on it.  But that's not what you're actually envisioning, is it? What you're talking about is the creation of _derivative works_ -- which are not actually innovative. They are simply tweaks on an existing theme. It's like the difference between an iPhone and a cheap Chinese knockoff of an iPhone versus the difference between an iPhone and an implant in your brain that allows you to telepathically communicate with other people that have the same implant. One is a derivative work, the other is actual innovation, completely unlike anything that had hitherto existed in history.  And that's the whole problem with using buzzwords like "innovation". They are overused so much that they reach the point of losing all meaning. You clearly have forgotten what actual innovation is if you think that the video montage of internet memes and Markiplier videos that you showed represent "innovation". They are derivation in its simplest and purest form.  Copyright might impinge on the ability to make cheap derivations of an existing property, but it doesn't prevent the invention of whole new technologies. To wit: telephone (and earlier, telegraph) wires were technologies that were under copyright at the time that the internet was invented, but that fact didn't stop anyone from dialing up in the early days of the internet. Quite the opposite, actually: the fact that there was a widespread wireline network spread all over the world formed a leverage point that allowed the internet to spring into existence without needing to develop huge amounts of additional infrastructure. If the copyright system were really as onerous as you make it out to be, that wouldn't have been possible.  The lesson of history is that copyright protects mostly entertainment products and legitimate scientific inventions without actually limiting further scientific and technological advancement. But that argument wouldn't sell to the public, so instead, you make a different, more fallacious (and mendacious) argument that copyright impedes innovation, which is demonstrably not true. :P You said it yourself: the US has the one of the world's most stringent copyright systems...and also undeniably the world's most innovative and productive economy. Almost everything that comprises the gossamer web of "modernity" as we know it was invented in the US in the past 100 years, and the rate of innovation is increasing over time, not decresing, even as the term of a copyright has also been increased. How can you possibly hope to account for those phenomena with your model of innovation that posits that more restrictive copyright leads to less innovation? Unfortunately, the way copyright laws presently exist, public domain day will never come for many works produced after 1977 due to the lack of notice and registration requirements.  I'd have no problem with giving works automatic copyright protection without a notice or registration requirement, nor with allowing copyrights to be renewed for a very long time--even indefinitely--if long-term protection required registration, and very-long-term protection required paying renewal fees on an escalating scale, but it seems ludicrous to protect works for 70 years past the life of the author without requiring any paperwork to even establish who the author is.  For the concept of "public domain" to be meaningful, it must be possible for someone who has a work and can establish that it was published long ago to be able to determine the work's copyright status.  If someone in 2120 finds a painting with an inscription "Elizabeth Smith 1980", and knows nothing about the painter beyond the name, they should be entitled to assume the painting has lapsed into the public domain; unfortunately, without knowing whether the painter was one of the countless Elizabeth Smiths that will have died between 1980 and 2049, or someone else who died in that time after having used Elizabeth Smith as a pseudonym, it would be impossible to be sure the painter hadn't managed to live until the year 2050 (which would be entirely plausible if they were 20 years old when they produced the painting).  If a company receives enough value from a 95-year-old copyright that it would be worth $1,000,000/year to keep renewing it, I'd have no objection to them spending $1,000,000/year to renew it as long as they want.  If, however, a company has so little interest in the copyright of a work that they can't even be bothered to register it within 30 years, however, the societal benefit of forbidding anyone else from doing anything with it for the next 60 would seem far smaller than the benefit of letting others find whatever value they can.

My problem with super long copyrights is that companies can just sit on them, taking them out of circulation for no real public good.  I've got a solution that I advocate for... shorten the term of copyright back down to something reasonable, like 25 years, but then allow companies to to extend it by setting a value on it that they have to pay taxes on.  The way to make sure they set the tax value fairly is to run it like a horse race... a claiming race in particular.  Say you have a copyright that is about to expire.  It generates revenue similar to what $1 million dollars would in the market, so you assess it's value at $1 million dollars and pay a tax based on that value.  We'd have to tinker with the tax rate to find the right balance for that tax, but ideally it should reach a stability point where it makes it so people set the value of their property rights appropriately... because if they don't set it appropriately someone else can come in and snatch it out from under them.  If you set the value at $1 million and someone else sets it at $1.1 million, they can just hand you the cash and now they own the rights.  At any point though, instead of paying the tax you can just say, "Okay, it's public domain now".  There is a value in making it public domain... you no longer have to pay tax to maintain the IP rights to it and you can still use it.  Of course, so can everyone else.  If someone really wants to buy exclusive rights to your property you have that leverage... that you could just make it public, as a bargaining chip... but of course, all they are losing is exclusivity.  So, if you look a the different players in this scenario-  -Copyright owners still get 25 years.  If they are willing to pay the tax and set the value of their work high enough so it doesn't get sniped they get to keep exclusive rights as long as they want.  If they do get sniped they get a payday because the company that sniped it is setting the value higher than current owner said it was worth- and probably enough higher to make sure another company doesn't come along and outbid them. -People who want to use works that are more than 25 years old can... the price if they want to buy it outright is already set.  They can, of course, still negotiate with the current owner for lesser rights like one time use or whatever.  The original owner may actually be more keen to do that, since they can use that money to keep paying the tax. -The public should, as long as you set the tax rate correctly, should see two benefits.... lots of stuff will go into the public domain a lot sooner.  Owners won't want to have to pay tax on something that isn't making them money.  Secondly, big companies will want to keep their flagship IP, so they should be willing to pay a lot in extra taxes which we plebes can use  to pay for things like universal healthcare (or Viacom healthcare?), free higher education, better schools, and other social programs (and maybe some tax relief for low income households?).    The downsides would be that big companies can keep their IP.  You could, if you wanted, set an absolute sunset, but you don't need to.  There is a public good in collecting tax revenue that can be used for helping people.  Companies that hold copyrights should constantly be going through their IP portfolios and those of their rivals for works that they want to keep, sell, licence or just release into the public domain.  Remember, anything you release into the public domain you can still use... you just don't own exclusive rights anymore.
