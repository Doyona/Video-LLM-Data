First off, will say, I love your content, Ren!  Just a little heads-up on King James grammar (from someone who has done years of Shakespeare study). I comment only because I figure you might like to know what the "correct" uses are, even if you choose to not care and continue winging-it. Your content is funny and charming either way, so it's completely up to you. üôÇ  A) "Thine" and "Thy" are both 2nd person (speaking to another person) possessive forms, equivalent to "your." So it'd be correct to say, for example, "Thine ears have heard my speech" or "Thy king is pleased with thee." It is not 1st person (speaking of yourself) possessive; for that you'd still say "My/Mine ears have heard thee."  B) "Thou" is the 2nd person form when it is the subject of a sentence, equivalent to "you." So, for example, "Thou art a knave and a squab" or "Thou goest forth throughout the kingdom."   C) "Thee" is the 2nd person form when it is the object of a sentence, i.e. when the action is being done to the other person, also used in place of "you.' For example, in "I have given thee this royal patterned shield," "thee" is the person to which you (the subject) gave the shield.  And that is all for my little unsolicited KJ grammar lesson. If I recall, 'thine' (and thy) means 'your' and 'yours'. 'Thee' is used for the object of a sentence, and 'thou' is the subject of the sentence. For example -> Thou art here = you are here, He gave it to thee = he gave it to you. The difference between thy and thine has to do with the word after; thy is used before a consonant and thine is used before a vowel.
