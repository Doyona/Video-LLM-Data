So, if I'm understading this right, as electricity is expelled, it branches out to equal paths of least resistance. The further it travels down one path, the more energy will follow it down that path. It's not so much that the other branches hit a proverbial brick wall so much as they encounter _some_ resistance, and in that time, one or more paths end up traveling far enough that energy stops going to those other branches because the energy has to follow the existing arcs.  If we imagine a room full of air as a room full of various viscous materials, and the path the electricity takes like someone trying to walk through it in a full-body suit, you'd have points where the person found patches of thicker material or outright solid objects, but a path would form. That's the path the electricity wants to take when the next electrical charge is expelled. But because the electricity heated up the air, the pressure changes so the path isn't exactly the same because other gases are being pushed into the space due to the hot air moving. Sort of like if the viscous room analogy were allowed to settle a bit before the next person went through, the path might not be exactly the same because some of the thicker material might encroach more on the path, or pool on the ground making it more difficult to lift your feet with each step.  It all appears random because we can't see the gases in the air that might be affecting the path that the electrical arcs take, and because the electricity itself is changing the air it's discharged into, that _further_ contributes to the random nature of the paths the electrical arcs take. As a Plasma Technology student, here are some thoughts on the video: 2:33 There might be two things at play here: As Medi mentioned, there is an ionized channel in the air and some of those ions/electrons might still be present by the time the next arc hits, reducing the required energy. There also exists something called metastables. A metastable is an excited atom/molecule that can't emit light (due to quantum mechanics). These metastables have quite a long life-time and are, because of their stored energy, easier to ionize. So the ionization energy of that channel decreases. These effects are then counteracted by convection (aka hot air rises), but it's a much much slower effect. 10:56 Yes, air can indeed blink that fast, even faster still. The lifetime of most excited states is in the range of a few to tens of nanoseconds. In plasma science, the most common frequency is 13.56 MHz, even there the light emission follows the shape of the voltage really closely. Optical spectroscopy is one of the most common diagnostics for us. 13:29 Here you can clearly see the effect of convection, which creates this nice arc shape 14:20 This is a really important detail here and it's really interesting for to actually see this. In atmospheric pressure electrical breakdown mainly happens via the so-called streamer mechanism. At these pressures, the electron density gets so big, that electric field that these electrons generate gets so big, the whole effect gets self-sustaining. A so-called streamer forms, which consists of a streamer head with a large number of electrons and a conductive streamer channel. Once the streamer hits the other electrode, there is a highly conductive channel between the two electrodes, essentially shorting the high voltage. This leads to these massive sparks. A streamer typically travels at the speed of a million meters per second. So assuming a 5 mm gap, that makes it 5ns to reach the other side. It is really remarkable that it's still visible here. 17:20 at a time resolution of less than 500ns you still can't really tell, if all of the gaps ignite at the same time or not. Electrical breakdowns can happen at much faster timescales. The scientific method to investigate this, would be to use a so called ICCD camera. These cameras can reach exposure times as low as 1ns. However, they can't shoot continuously. So you need a repeatable way to precisely trigger the discharge and then use a so-called delay generator to sync that to the camera.
