I work in a distribution centre. There are 2 things to understand about how pallets are organised, because it's not by their location on the shelves at the store.   1. They're organised by weight. The heavy stuff goes on the bottom, the light stuff goes on the top. This makes the pallet more stable. Putting the heavy things on top may also crush some of the lighter things, for example you would not put lots of pasta sauce on top of crisps (chips for you Americans). The heavier things will therefore be located at the front of the picking aisle, and it's a 1 way system.   2. By shape/size. There are certain patterns to how products will fit together. It makes your job much harder and slower if you try to play a game of tetris, much easier and faster if you already know where everything is going. Typically, you will spilt the pallet into zones (‚Öì, ‚Öõ, etc) and after enough time you'll be familiar with all the products enough to know in which of these zones they fit. This is the part that takes longest for a picker to learn, so many of the newer pickers mess this up and create weird looking pallets.  I also used to work in a supermarket, and we used to change the location of our stock fairly frequently, probably more than you'd expect. The spots for the products change enough to where the distribution centre can't really be expected to update to product placement on the pallets to that degree.   I can't speak for the automated distribution centres, since I don't have experience there. Ok, more than 35 years of grocery retail here.... and while Krogers may do it that way, most small stores don't.  1) Independent stores order manually most of the time. Large grocery loads are not scanned in; the dollar amount is tracked for inventory. 2) Because humans build the pallets at the warehouses, shorts (missing items) mispicks (the wrong item selected) etc are common, as are damaged cases shipped in.  3) With small chains, the stores aren't uniform. Hell, with some LARGE chains, they aren't all set the same (Market Basket in New England is a good example) therefore pallets aren't built uniformly. As for small chains using a wholesaler, they aren't even built consistently from delivery to delivery. Pallets are mixed items, and the mix is not consistent.   As for out of stocks, they can last weeks.... in the case of Covid, months, with them only being IN stock for a week or two.  The theory here is nice... it does not reflect reality for most chains, though the big four may well be doing it that way

I work at a regional midwest supermarket and, while I'm not sure if this is true universally, where I work there are actually a few different ways distribution works.  While the majority of products come from a centralized warehouse, as mentioned here, certain brands (Coca-Cola, Pepsi, Nabisco, and Frito-Lay to name a few) will actually control their own distribution and deliver directly to a store from their own warehouses instead of through that store's primary distribution center in a method called Direct Store Delivery, or DSD. These tend to be extremely fast moving items that is unfeasible to reach the store in a timely fashion otherwise. For example, at the store I work at Pepsi would typically deliver 8-12 pallets of their products 4 times every week. These items are also ordered, delivered, and stocked solely by employees of that company as opposed to the store's employees. I work as someone who checks these items in to make sure that the company is actually delivering and stocking the items they say they are, as they are inventoried and billed as soon as they enter the store.  Another way items get delivered is it is actually mailed using UPS or FedEx, though that's typically either much slower moving products or products that need to reach the store extremely fast after it's produced, and never grocery. I stock shelves at a grocery store. This video is a really good explanation of how the system is supposed to work.  It's far from perfect though. My store uses CAO, computer assisted ordering. The system tracks how many units of a given item we have in stock, how many we're supposed to have, and how many we're expected to sell before the next order. If the number in stock is ever less than the number of expected sales, it automatically orders more from the warehouse. We do periodic maintenance walks within each department, which is mostly just walking the isle, looking for anything that looks over or under stocked, and adjusting the numbers accordingly. That accounts for damages, shoplifting, mispicks (when the warehouse accidentally sends us the wrong thing), and other "shrink" as it's called.  When an item goes on display (the end caps that we change every week or so) that item will go into display status in the system, so it'll order more to account for the extra shelf space.  Unfortunately sometimes people forget to take stuff off of display status and the warehouse keeps sending double orders. Or sometimes the warehouse workers' brains stop working for a bit and they put heavy stuff on top of light stuff and the pallet falls over while we're taking it off the truck. In my case, my manager is kind of an idiot and does maintenance walls on my isle and just zeroes stuff out instead of checking to see if we have some in the back.  The Cao system though is simply not designed with the needs of the stores in mind. It's designed for the warehouse, to move inventory out of the warehouse, specifically. So if the warehouse gets overstocked on something, they'll just force it out to the stores, whether we need it or not. Screw it, it's the store's problem now.  It was really bad in 2020 when warehouse workers were all getting sick, so they started pulling people from the stores to work the warehouse and of course they had even less idea what they were doing than the usual warehouse crew. You make a relatively major error that, as someone relatively familiar with grocery store logistics, bothers me quite a bit.  You excluded direct store delivery (DSD) from the logistics model you laid out. This is a critical mistake as it‚Äôs how a ton of products are delivered and shelved in stores nationwide. Major manufacturers like Pepsi, coke, nabisco, etc. have teams of salesmen and delivery drivers that ensure stores are supplied with products.  The fact that you used nabisco as an example when discussing how products are delivered from manufacturers to distribution centers and then to stores is what made me decide to comment. Nabisco is a DSD vendor which means that the manufacturer delivers merchandise directly to the store, bypassing the grocer‚Äôs distribution center.    I‚Äôll explain the way this works (in most cases) using nabisco (mondelez) as an example: 1. Products are manufactured at Mondelez factories. 2. Products are shipped to regional Mondelez distribution centers. 3. Product is delivered to stores by Mondelez drivers and is then checked into store inventory management systems. 4. Mondelez sales and merchandising employees arrive at stores and stock product on store shelves. 5. Those same sales and merchandising employees use Mondelez inventory management software and sales projection software to order the next delivery.  Depending on the product, companies who participate in store DSD programs will deliver and merchandise product anywhere from 2-5 times a week. Mission tortilla vendors, for instance, service most stores 2 times a week, while coke services most stores 5 times a week. Additionally, some companies employ sales, merchandising, and drivers as all separate positions, some combine them into just one position and some combine the sales and merchandising jobs into one position. Mission has one sales/driver/merchandiser do the whole job for each store while coke has a separate employee for each aspect of the job.
