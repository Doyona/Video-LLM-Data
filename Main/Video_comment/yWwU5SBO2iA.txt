A quick tip I can suggest for meal prep is to consider prepping ingredients/components for later assembly, rather than fully made meals. It's a simple way to help with variety, because you can mix and match the components. And you can make things more interesting by changing up spices, sauces, etc. Like if you roast squash on a baking tray, coat one half in smokey savoury spices like paprika and chipotle, and the other half in cinnamon and butter. Lemon juice, caramelized onions, stock, chopped ginger, etc. Like with your millions of onions, you can chop them and caramelize them then freeze into small portions (you can use an ice cube tray or just portion into lil containers) then add one portion to a meal your cooking too add a ton of flavour without the effort. And you can take the same meal but change up the flavours by using a different prepped aromatic.

Taz, I think an important aspect of meal prep to remember is that you don't have to prep everything so that it's ready to eat straight away. The quinoa you cooked up with the chickpea and spinach curry is an excellent example of this. The salmon you want to do could be another - since it doesn't take much time to cook salmon, don't cook that ahead of time. Instead, plan out the things you might want to eat with that salmon and prep those instead.  On the prep days, you should try to do the steps of a meal that are the most time-consuming. Generally this means prepping your veggies - peeling, chopping, blanching, etc. Just be mindful that some things do not store well in the freezer, and some things only store well in some forms but not others (e.g. raw potatoes are generally fine, but boiled don't tend to do well. Leafy greens or tomatoes don't store well due to water content if you want to eat them raw, but if you're planning to stew or puree them anyways they are completely fine to freeze).  Other things to consider are slow-cooked meals like chili, bean or lentil dishes, soups, certain sauces, etc. Speaking of sauces, those are also a great thing to prep. It depends on the sauce, but generally they are easy to make in bulk, easy to store, and have a variety of applications. Things like pickles are also easy to make at home and can be used as condiments, side dishes, or ingredients in their own right. I really like pickled onions and carrots with richer meals, personally

Multi-tasking tips --  First thing you should setup are foods that can can cook on their own without much effort (i.e. coffee, rice, boiling water, toaster oven). Set audio timer for these things so they don't overcook or burn.   While these things are cooking, prep for next dish. This can include cutting veggies, taking out spices, cuting meat etc. Have all of them nearby.   Next check on your auto-cook items from before. Turn off or plate items that are done.   Finally, cook the active dish on preferably medium /low-medium heat by using the prepped ingredients. This ensures that nothing gets burned. By the time this dish is cooked (usually minutes), the remaining auto-cook dishes will probably also be done.   It's all about timing different parts of the dish and keeping a mental note of it all. It definitely takes some practice, but with some effort and time, anyone can do it! It's a matter of getting in your own flow state and trying different things out to see what works for you.
