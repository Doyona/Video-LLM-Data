As a retired builder I have to point out some important errors in your flooring installation.   When floors are installed, they are nailed on one side only, the tongue, to allow for wood movement.  In a room this size there should also be a half inch gap by the walls and not tight to the walls.  The gap is then covered with baseboard.  Also the choice of pine.  Pine was used in older homes when pine trees grew to four feet diameters.  The heartwood was harder.  They don't let trees grow that big anymore.  I'd suggest OSMO wood finish, available at Lee Valley,  It has hard waxes that penetrate and can be reapplied  anytime with a bit of cleaning and light sanding.

Regarding trench-digging in excessively rocky ground, folks:  If you can't dig down, you can always mound up!  This does leave you with a bit of a berm to deal with, but you can pile on and tamp down more dirt to extend the slopes into something more gradual and less noticeable.  If you're moving heavy machinery over these berms, you can bury some of those rocks on either side of the pipeline along with lengthy pieces across it, braced from rock to rock, before packing on the dirt.  If you do't have to worry about crossing the line, another possibility is building a drystone wall over/around the conduit, especially if you're trying to clear rocks from a field for future planting.  A third might be to build a wattle-woven fence, though that would be a bit more difficult to weave.
