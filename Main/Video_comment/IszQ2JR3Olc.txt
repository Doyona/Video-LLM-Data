I think that an important distinction is that just because the process of decaffeinating coffee is "destructive" to the chemistry of the beans, doesn't mean it has to be "bad." Roasting coffee beans is a "destructive" process. You are changing the chemical structure of the coffee bean in a way that is irreversible. This does not mean that unroasted coffee which hasn't gone through any "destructive" processes is a better experience for you, even brewing the coffee (extracting the flavor), is destructive. This is also true for simply cooking food. Cooking a scrambled egg is destructive, but I'd argue that a scrambled egg is a superior breakfast food than a whole raw egg.  A "Destructive" process (in Chemistry), by definition, is a procedure that changes the composition of something in a way that is (non-trivially) irreversible.  The process of decaffeinating may yield a subjectively "worse" taste than not doing it, however, it has nothing to do with being "destructive" or not, these are just buzzwords. It is possible that in the future, someone will invent a method of decaffeination that will actually yield a subjectively better end product than the regular coffee beans, in the same way that we've improved regular coffee in recent years through many new discoveries and inventions.

They comprise multiple phenols, but some scientists only consider the class of polyphenols with >12 phenolic hydroxides and 500-4000Da molecular weight.
