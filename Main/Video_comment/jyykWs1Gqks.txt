I build a lot of things in the wild, which includes a lot of hand carving. There are several secrets to it that will make the carving tool you bought work better for you.  The first thing is that each piece of wood has its own character in the grains so that it will make terrible chips and splinters if you carve it in some directions but it will carve nicely and smoothly in other directions. Just experiment with each piece of wood and, if one direction doesn't carve smoothly, try another one until you find the directions in which the wood carves nicely. Eventually, you'll start noticing various patterns in the kind of wood you're using that will help you figure this out better when you first start carving but, even if you don't notice that and chip the wood on your first try, you can usually fix this once you find the direction that works. In my experience, it is usually perpendicular to the grains of wood and from the outer part of the branch towards the center with the direction often shifting at the pith (which is the center of the branch and I've only carved wood I've cut down myself so I don't know how to tell this on pre-cut wood).  Another thing is that you don't want to try to remove too much wood for every cut so you want to make many shallow cuts rather than trying to quickly remove the wood you want to carve away because trying to remove too much at once will also cause it to chip and splinter instead of resulting in a smooth surface. If you do need to cut a lot of it off, use an axe or a saw or be extremely patient slowing carving that part off. Personally, I do parts like that with my hatchet.  Also, make sure your tools are as sharp as you can get them because sharper knife is safer than a dull one because a dull one is more likely to catch and then pop off of the wood and onto anything in its path after that. I've caught myself with my knives before and even my folding saw and they'll give you quite an impressive cut if they catch you. So you also want to think about where your knife might go if this does happen and then make sure either not to carve in the direction of any of your body parts or, if you do carve towards yourself, make sure that you have something preventing the knife from popping off and cutting you This can be accomplished by certain positions of holding your knife and the piece of wood or by having something that will stop the knife before it can cut you. There are a number of excellent tutorials about all of this on YouTube, which is how I learned.  Once you get the hang of it, carving is actually pretty fun. It does make somewhat of a mess, though, because you end up having loads of wood shavings everywhere and they screw up the vacuum if you try to clean them up that way so you want to either be outside or in a place where you can sweep the shavings up. In any case, that machine will be a lot of fun once you get the hang of it and I think you guys will enjoy all the things you can make with it as much as I enjoy all the things I've made with my hand tools.
