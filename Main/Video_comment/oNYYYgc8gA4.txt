I really love the ludo-narrative going on with Tinkaton and Gigaton Hammer.  Like yes, it's a good move and balanced appropriately, but think about what its specific mechanics reflect what it represents in the game's world:  Gigaton Hammer hits _really_ hard, as you might expect from just looking at the object it represents -- Tinkaton's massive freaking hammer.  But Tinkaton's attack stat _isn't_ high, meaning the hammer is specifically represented by the move Gigaton Hammer, and _everything else_ that Tinkaton can do is represented by its otherwise mediocre offenses.  Furthermore, the since the move can't be used consecutively, it's like the hammer is difficult to wield... which makes sense. (Look at it!)  But it's not like Hyper Beam or Giga Impact, which take so much effort that the user has to rest for a turn. No, Tinktaton's hammer is tough for it to wield, but _only_ the hammer. It doesn't _exhaust_ itself with a hammer swing -- it just takes time to maneuver it for another strike. In the meantime, Tinkaton can do other things, but its low raw offense represents that it's not going to be making *that* much of an impact without using its hammer.  So Tinkaton, as far as I know, is the most drastic example of a Pokemon whose design representation is split significantly between its stats and its signature move, all elegantly integrated as balance restrictions.  Its stats?  Not that big, because Tinkaton is just a lil pink thing.  Its Hammer move?  _Big,_ because big hammer, and _slow_ be because... big hammer.  As a cherry on top, as far as I know, Gigaton Hammer is the first move that can't be used twice in a row as a specific restriction. I mean, there are other moves that themselves take 2 turns (either by charge-up or by recharge), moves that are _unwise_ to use consecutively, and a handful whose more specific restrictions imply that it can't be used twice in a row (like Steel Roller, which requires terrain and destroys it)... but only Gigaton Hammer _specifically_ says "you can't use this twice in a row".  So they added an entirely new type of restriction _just_ to balance Gigaton Hammer in a way that also makes sense for what it represents in Tink's design!

6:56 Feel free to check my math (not my best work), but I think the average # of hits for Population Bomb is 5.862 with a standard deviation of .898. So 95% of attacks should hit between 4.07 and 7.66 times. This doesn't reflect the probability completely accurately though as 10 hits should occur about 34.9% of the time. The probability is not actually approximated by the binomial distribution due to this. If we take the condition that the number of hits is less than 10, the conditional expected value becomes 3.647. So in short the move is pretty good because the risk is low and reward is high.
