Just an FYI: On the early Toyota pickup and van fuel systems with the 'door-type' airflow meter, the engine needs to be cranking to activate the fuel pump solenoid. Once the air door is pulled open by engine vacuum, it will continue to feed power to the pump relay and maintain fuel pressure. That's why when you taped up the tube between the throttle body and the AFM, it would continue to run. BTW, the big fat screw sticking out of the top of the throttle body about 1/4" or so is the base idle speed screw. In most cases it will be about level with the throttle body or a little below, which would equate to about 750 rpm. the engine will only run for a few second with the AFM disconnected because the COR allows power to the fuel pump during cranking (key in start position) and when the key is in the RUN position the AFM needs to be open (sucking air) to send power to the COR to run the Fuel Pump.  If the AFM is unhooked, then the engine will just run until the fuel supply provided during cranking is exhausted (couple of seconds)  In your case, with the fuel pump getting power from an independent source, none of that should be an issue.  The AFM does a couple other things as well, it primarily contains a potentiometer that measures physical air flow for the ECM as well as measuring the AIR TEMPERATURE, both of those things will help it run properly and are probably why it doesn't run well with the AFM disconnected.  So long as your fuel pump is set up completely independent of the old wiring circuit, then fuel delivery shouldn't be the issue. You may have a lean condition. common example is issues with the intake tube before the throttle body but after the AFM cracking or not sealing and letting in too much unmetered air.
