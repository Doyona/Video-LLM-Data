They are cutting out a LOT of extra "ground and pound" comparitive to the earlier years. At the same time, we are seeing more "walk away" KO's, perhaps BECAUSE the victorious fighter knows he's done enough to get the stoppage?  UFC (and the othe organisations) are a SERIOUS contender for a finite number of people who want to pursue combat sports professionally.   (2) Either way, a protected fighter can take a rematch. It's easy to argue that Vargas was a second away from a stunning counter, or whatever version of events you might like to construct...but ask yourself this: If a friend of yours at school got into a fight and was losing like this, would you have waited to step in? Or would you have said "he got you this time, there's always tomorrow"  (3) No crowd noise. Referees are human beings and the roar of the crowd is hard to block out. In a quiet arena the ref's perceptions are entirely focussed AND enhanced by the fact that he can hear can hear the breathing of the losing fighter, hear the sound of the blows. A ref standing on one side can't be sure how clean a hook to the OTHER side landed...or did it hit the glove?   Sometimes, when we watch clips of beatings that clearly went on too long, the only credible explanation (other than sadism) is that the referee has stopped officiating momentarily and is watching, along with the baying crowd.  (4) I really belive that a rule change would be good for boxing: The three point round. Instead of boxing for three minutes, this round could have been stopped at the time the ref went in.Three points to Benn. We would still see fights ended by clean knockouts, but no more pummeling knockouts which, in my non-medical opinion, do far more damage long term.  In order for this to work, I'd have four doctors watching. If three press a button, the ref gets the nod from his earpiece (nearly every other sports' ref wears one) and the crowd are alerted by a Lights system. Then the ref have the final say, but the OK to do it was not his (potentially biased and or bribed) decision.
