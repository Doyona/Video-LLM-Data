I think there are couple reasons for retaining walls to fail at such high rate compared to other types of constructions. For one you have very conflicting priorities. Everybody wants it to be as cheap as possible (retaining wall probably the last thing you want to be overpaying on) but also wants it to look nice (straight -- the last thing you want when building a retaining wall on the cheap). Another problem is that you are dealing with highly variable element (soil) that can dramatically change properties depending on weather but also you frequently it is brought from somewhere else after the wall is designed meaning as a designer you don't exactly know what's it going to be and how well it is going to be compacted/drained. And then, let's just admit retaining walls aren't the sexiest projects out there. They are frequently part of something bigger, a side to the main dish. Hi Grady.  Great episode.  I live in the area you featured in the opening and commute up NJSR 295 almost every day.  If you look carefully at the aerial images you will see large expanses of open space in the background.  that is a cemetery - a huge source of infiltration for stormwater.  What you cant see in the images is  the fact that this area is part of a long belt of clay deposits that run from southwest to north east NJ, roughly along the NJ Turnpike.  These clays are what's known as 'acid producing' as they are buried, sufidic marine clays.  When exposed to air and water, the sulfides react and produce sulfuric acid.  It is not treatable with any amount of lime and must be kept unexposed during construction and reburied.  The acid is strong - ph around 2 and can attack any metal or concrete and will kill almost any vegetation it comes in contact with.   The other part of this scenario is the fact that the clays are highly impermeable.  They used to be mined and used as a liner and cap material for landfills.  The clays are set in layers of alternating sand and clay.  During initial stages of removing vegetation from all the slopes along 295, the slopes were left barren for a long time.  The slope cuts caused the clay layers to daylight  in this area.  And many many times, large seeps were visible in different places along the slope.  In winter you could easily see them because they froze.  I think the combination of the acid clay as well as the seeps with the enormous open source of rainwater infiltration probably undermined the wall.  The basic wall design was a vertical layer cake of the panels on the sides connected to each other on opposite sides of the road with a geogrid which when loaded, would act to pull the panels inward or at least hold them tightly.  there are many layers of this as the wall goes up.  There is not much else behind those panels but earth and geogrids acting in tension with the applied earthen load.  Anyhow the clay and those seeps are major factors in the construction out there.  it will be very interesting to see what the final forensic report comes out with. I did not see in the vvideo or in the comments any mention of the most common type of retaining wall, home basement walls. These are usually of block construction, 8" wide with a 2X6 plate on top flush with the outside surface of the block. Unfortunately, on theweight bearing sides of the house (where the floor joists rest) this adds the weight load of the house asymetrically to the outside of the wall leaving the inside of the block wall free of any imposed load. Basement walls will then separate along the horizontal morter joints as the soil burden presses in. I have closed those cracks back up by driving wooden wedges under the floor joists bringing part of the load down onto the inside of the block wall. Both the building contractor and the building inspector specifically approved the 2X6 plate on the 8" wide block construction.

There is an additional element to the "retaining wall" that is constructed by tying a wall to the soil with geotextile fabrics, etc. In that type of construction the earth must be compacted to a specified density. The wall doesn't support the contained soil, the soil supports the wall, which is actually just a weather face for the geostructure consisting of layers of compacted soil with interleaving layers of geotextile  fabric. Additionally, there must be a curtain of gravel behind the wall for the full height and drainage pipes at the bottom of the gravel curtain to prevent hydrostatic pressures from acting on the soil side of the wall. That is exactly the type of construction that I used on the rear of my hill side property to extend a flat yard to the downslope property line. The "wall" height is up to fourteen feet. During construction I had to constantly monitor the workers doing the compaction, geotextile placement and wall block laying. They repeatedly tried to speed up the process by laying too deep a layer of soil before compaction, which was done with a "jumping jack" type of compactor. To properly compact the soil to the required density no more than six inches of loose soil at at time could be placed before compaction. Additionally, even though constructed in a dry environment, the rear yard, which is about twenty-five feet wide, contains drains spaced every twenty feet along the length to prevent the soil from becoming saturated with water.

A Secant retaining wall was built into the emergency spillway system while they were rebuilding the primary spillway  at the Oroville Dam after both failed in 2017. I don't understand all of the engineering in the rebuild but it was extensive.  During the 2017 emergency flooding, the heavily damaged primary spillway could not take on maximum capacity output without sustaining more damage so they slowed the gates and allowed the emergency spillway to top over to help drain flood waters. It had never been used and therefore untested in the dam's history. The run off scoured off the topsoil and revealed the inherent weaknesses in the  bedrock beneath as being highly fractured, unstable and susceptible to erosion by the rapidly draining water. The run off was causing rapid headwater erosion in the fractured rock down the slope of the emergency spillway  potentially jeopardizing  the dam's integrity so they had to stop the top-over and open the primary floodgates further. The emergency spillway was later redesigned in the repair phase with extensive reinforced concreate capping the fractured bedrock and a secant wall added near its base to stabilize the rock slope.
