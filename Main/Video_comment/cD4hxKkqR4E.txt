The reason why ccTLDs exist is to allow countries to manage their own domain name registrations. Without it, we'll all be relying on a single naming registration organisation who had to try to satisfy the needs and demands of every countries on earth. That is in essence going to be the American defaultism again, and we'd just get a fractured internet as countries that want to do their own thing might just develop their own system instead.  With ccTLDs, ICANN doesn't really have to set a policy in regards to how the ccTLDs are administered. Each country are free to manage their ccTLDs as they see fit, whether that's to limit registration to just local entities or to open it up for international registration, those policies are defined by the country's government, not by ICANN.  The point of TLDs or ccTLDs, in essence is to show under whose naming authorities that the website is registered under. ICANN doesn't set any policies regarding to how a top level domain registrar are administered.

There might be one more reason to explain why .us TLD is not as popular.  When you register any domain, you are asked to provide a set of contact information (usually up to 4 of them - Registrant, Administative, Technical, Billing). After you register a domain, all the information about it like its Registrar company, expiration date, nameservers and contact details can be found in a public database called Whois. Since not many people actually want their contact details to be public, there are services like WhoisGuard, which replace the actual information with a generic set of details.  When it comes to ccTLDs, most of them do not support WhoisGuard. Nevertheless, each TLD displays the information in Whois differently (sometimes it is only available on the Registry website) and the vast majority hide almost all of the contact details of the domain owner. However, .us Registry (which manages all .us domains) does things differently. They require all this information to be valid and public while they also don't allow WhoisGuard or a similar service to be used with any .us domain. This is a well-known situation amongst particular Internet users.  Basically, when a new .us domain is registered, they somehow track this information in Whois and then start spamming and calling the domain owner to the point which makes many people regret getting a .us domain in the first place. There is a way around this, of course, by replacing your actual number and email address with a dedicated set of contacts. Unfortunately, as a consequence, a lot of people either cancel .us domains shortly afterwards or avoid those registrations altogether.

More importantly, the "extended" TLDs, .info, .dog and so on, are not universally supported because they're not part of the original standard and for the same reason there are some of them that only work in the countries where they were created. Thus sites that use these TLD typically have to be registered as an alias of a "standard" domain, or alternatively point to a sub-domain (which in turn physically points to a folder on a primary domain) , in order to be universally accessible from anywhere. Strictly speaking (yes, I *am* 'that guy'), a C.V., or curriculum vitae, isn't the same as a resume.   The CV is a list of EVERYTHING you've done; every school you've attended, every certification you've earned, every job you worked at, every activity you've engaged in - it's basically your life history.    In contrast, a resume is an edited summary of your C.V. that's submitted to a prospective employer that lists only that part of your work/school/etc history *that is relevant to the job you're applying for*.  For example, you wouldn't put down babysitting experience before you turned 18 on an application to be a company's bookkeeper; on the other hand, you WOULD list that you'd taken a part-time job during uni working in the campus library, as the latter relates more to the accounting work you are applying for. (obviously, you'd note that you are a CPA prominently in the resume ;) )  A resume should be as short as possible, rarely over three pages, and focused on what the employer is interested in, but even a school student's C.V. already might be quite lengthy with activities and experience.

If your domain name server removes an entry for a website (e.g. because of government censorship), you can get around that block by adding the name to your hosts file.
