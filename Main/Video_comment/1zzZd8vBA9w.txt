To add to some of the other comments on the ebikes. I spent 10 years working for a bikeshop during the rise of ebikes. I've seen tons of lower cost ebikes come through for repairs, many times there was nothing we could do to repair it, neither parts nor manuals were available. For your purposes, a quality folding bike would be a better option. To break this down in an easy to understand way from a bike guy perspective, a good regular bicycle with quality components will run you $1000 and up, preferably $2000. A good ebike motor system, just the system, will run you $2000 and up. You guys are going to depend on these in the wee corners of the world, where service on ebikes will not exist. Bicycles are like anything else, you get what you pay for. Good manufacturers of folding bikes are Tern, Dahon and Bike Friday. Go browse their websites and familiarize yourselves with what quality bicycles are like and how they are equipped. There is so much junk and fly by night companies in the ebike arena atm I would recommend getting an ebike from a dedicated bicycle company that has been around for years. They will stand behind the product, have a worldwide presence and be there when you need them. Feel free to reach out with any questions.

Lectric bikes are really great for a house, but they are heavy and unwieldly for a sailboat.  Brompton electric bikes are amazingly compact and super easy to move on and off of moving platforms.  I bought Lectric bikes (step-thru XP2.0) for my sailboat and then found this out.  Now I have bought a Brompton electric and it is a game changer.  They are expensive.  Since you are in Asia, you can buy a Harry Quinn folding electric bike for about 35% of the cost of the Brompton, however, be aware that there are many little things that make the Harry Quinn not as good as the Brompton.  It depends on your specific needs.  I think I will be trying to use 1 Brompton and 1 Harry Quinn on my sailboat to see how they work out.  I am 80% sure that I will end up with 2 Brompton electric bikes though. The BESV PSF1 is an alternative if you need to have bigger wheels.  It has 20 inch wheels.  The Brompton and the Harry Quinn both have 16 inch wheels.  The issue with the BESV is that while it is compact and light, it is not really designed to be carried in odd situations (like on a sailboat and dinghy).  The Brompton is the most easy to carry.  The Harry Quinn does fold up and lock in place, so it's also easy to handle.  The BESV is light, but does not lock up in a folding position that carries well.  The Lectric does not lock in a folding position at all and really requires 2 people to manage it safely. You mentioned your concern about island riding on difficult terrain.  The Lectric would be the best at this, but the Brompton is still quite capable, albeit sometimes a little more bumpy.  I've ridden the Brompton electric for long rides (40km) on cobble and rough roads and it was really fine.  The Brompton has better shock absorption than the Harry Quinn.  I do not have experience with the BESV on bumpy roads. In terms of carrying capacity.  The Lectric can carry a lot.  The Brompton can carry a lot.  Both of these have very good supply chains for luggage options.  The Harry Quinn is limited to the rear rack, it's actually quite limited in compatibility with bike bags.  I've been trying to build a modified front-bracket so that I can mount brompton bags on the Harry Quinn, but so far I have been unsuccessful.  I have to modify the front brake to make it work.  My HQ mod efforts have really highlighted just how very well designed the Brompton bikes are.  This is why I think I will just end up with 2 Brompton electric bikes. There is a world more that I can share about international ebike laws.  No problems with the Brompton or HQ anywhere.

In terms of seriously looking to bring a couple of electric-assist bikes onboard, start with bikes that have been fully tested and certified. So many of these Chinese imports have chosen not to complete the proper UL certification. Also, as you travel around the globe, make sure you are selecting a company that can supply you with replacement parts shipped to wherever you land. So many of the Chinese importers have limited access to replacement parts and often the factory changes elements that render the original version obsolete without telling anyone.  Reach out to any of the companies you are looking at and ask if they support if they support the UL 2849 system certification. While you are at it, unless the bike is being delivered in someone's car, ask if they have full UNDOT 38.3 safe transport certification. These are the elements you will need to keep your boat safe from lithium challenges. Unless things have changed, I do not believe that Lectric has completed the UL certification process, for their Chinese drives and batteries.

E bikes are VERY dangerous due to "Thermal Runaway".  Thermal runaway is a chain reaction within a battery cell that can be very difficult to stop once it has started. It occurs when the temperature inside a battery reaches the point that causes a chemical reaction to occur inside the battery.  If the cause of excessive heat creation is not remedied, the condition will worsen. Internal battery temperature will continue to rise - causing battery current to rise - creating a domino effect. The rise in temperature in a single battery will begin to affect other batteries in close proximity, and the pattern will continue, thus the term â€œrunaway.â€  A LOT of care needs to be taken when charging.
