HDMI was never in the consumer's best interest.  Lured by the "convenience" of one plug, we've pretty much opted in to a world of draconian anti-consumerism.  Yay us!  A short of list of things HDMI did wrong:  1) Twisted pair.  This is, and always will be, a limiting factor to the bandwidth capability of the cable.  Fast signals like coax.  We've known this for ages.  Fast signals also like controlled impedance.  You know what does controlled impedance really well?  Alternatively, how about fiber optics?  Then we can reduce cable size, too.  On that note...  2)  Big thick cables and small connectors with no mechanical support.  So to solve that, let's make *smaller* connectors with big thick cables.  And then, even smaller ones!    3)  Integrated A/V.  This is hit-and-miss.  It would have been SO much more sensible to have separate (but perhaps bondable) A/V channels.  Then we wouldn't necessarily even need to worry about sending 4K / 8K through a receiver, unless you also want video source switching beyond what your TV has.  4)  DRM.  Enough is enough.  Seriously.  Nobody's going to vid-cap 2 hours of video when they can just rip the content in a way more convenient way.  And nobody would do _that_ if they could play it when they wanted to, how they wanted to, for a trivial price.  "But people wouldn't pay!"  Yes they would.  Some wouldn't, but they're not going to anyway.  If people stop paying for content, quality content goes away.  It's a self-solving problem.  Honesty exists, if you actually give it half a chance...  5)  It's yet another patent farm.  I would love to see a hardware equivalent of open-source software, where signaling protocols, interface specifications, and algorithms were all freely available to anyone who wants to make compliant devices.  Instead, we live in a world where every idea is patented, so at this point, we can't even create new interfaces from scratch because _some_ aspect of _any_ design is inherently going to infringe on _someone's_ patent.
