Somewhat related to this, another cartoon that fits in this category is My Little Pony. I know it's to be expected in a show that teaches friendship to kids, but the show writers did it in a different and more symbolic way than the ones mentioned in this video. They used nonhuman characters with differences representative of humans but without actually labeling them as such.   My Little Pony has multiple episodes where one or multiple characters express racism towards other species only to realize that those feelings are shallow and baseless after one or multiple experiences with a member of those species (ex. "Hearths Warming Eve" and "Bridle Gossip"). It's less about the presence of racism in societies and more about the fallout, how preconceived ideas about groups are often wrong and that labeling all members of a group a certain way always leads to incorrect perceptions and assumptions, which I thought the show writers did a good job at depicting.  Kids don't understand the complexities of real-world racism and how harmful it can be, so they're less likely to be able to sympathize with what they see in shows with humans. But with racial conflicts between groups of characters (like unicorns and pegasi and earth ponies) telling the same story about how judging others based on arbitrary characteristics can be harmful, children are able to grasp that concept and understand why it's a bad thing. And I think it's important that they're taught this way because they learn to sympathize with people in the real world who may face discrimination, just as they saw in My Little Pony with the ponies' negative reaction to seeing a friendly zebra character for the first time.  This isn't to say that racism approached in cartoons with humans isn't effective, just that I believe it's easier for children to understand when the characters aren't given human characteristics and therefore take the lessons at face value. My Little Pony is genuinely a great show that's done a good job at showing heavy issues such as racism and even self-harm at one point.  I didn't mean to write so much here but I feel as though the writers have approached these topics well and definitely deserve to be recognized :]
