So cool to see what I do on such a large scale and to recognize a lot of the same equipment in my own lab. For anyone more curious in how Cyclotrons work we inject hydrogen into the cyclotron's ion source which adds an electron to the hydrogen particles giving them a negative charge. The electron beam then circles around a tank under very high vacuum where it is controlled by magnets and RF. Eventually the electron beam hits an extractor made out of carbon that strips off the electrons, changing the polarity of the beam from negative to positive (proton beam). The change in polarity allows the magnet to fire the beam into a target of pressured 018 water which after being bombarded turns into the radioisotope F18 used by the chemists and pharmacists to make a number of different traces.

Such pneumatic systems are great: They are comparatively cheap, reliable, long lasting, efficient and fast. They are NOT for bulk-transport so if you need to move a lot of stuff look somewhere else. It is their simplicity that makes them work. The connection only needs to be a rather simple tube that needs to be decently smooth on the inside and can not bend too tightly - which can be done with cheap plastic pipes that last forever. The systems on each end are also really simple if it is used just for point-to-point connections (switches are available but mostly avoided due to complexity and problems they create). They are incredible fast too as there is nearly nothing that can go even close to that speed, let alone go that speed right to the place you want to send it to or where you are working.  I have seen them used in a university for physical data-transfer. Unplugging some hotswopable HDDs and sending them to the other side of the building and then plugging them in again only takes a couple of minutes, netting you effective speeds in the range of a couple hundred GB/second (doing that over cable would require some expensive networking-gear, high performance storage on both end and still be limited to datatransmission - so no "i'll send you over a pcb-prototype" ).  And they are fun.
