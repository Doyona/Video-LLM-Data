Y'know, the "joke" about tattooing neighborhood cats might not be 100% a joke. Hear me out.  One of the things many places have been doing as an attempt to control the wildly invasive, destructive, and dangerous feral housecat populations (seriously, THEY ARE A PROBLEM, DO NOT LET CATS OUTSIDE) is simple catch and release programs where you catch the ferals, spay/neuter them, and then release. But how do you easily find out which ones have already been treated without, well, cutting 'em open again? You could do ultrasounds and other such things, but that requires a LOT of handling, stress, and in the end, it's a huge and costly trouble. You could take the tip off their ear... But that's considered pretty barbaric by many, and also can happen naturally so it's not a perfect indicator. There's a lot of different things you can do, but one of the commonly agreed upon methods is... Tattoo them. You've already got the cat anesthetized and controlled while doing the surgery itself, so there's very little extra handling needed up front, and then all you have to do is check the crotch area for a tattoo showing through the fur in order to see that they've already been handled and are clear for release. It's not something that'd happen naturally, it's a clear indicator that almost anyone in those programs would recognize (or would quickly learn to after a few encounters), and it has no real impact on the cat. Perfect? Nah, still has the issue of not being visible from a distance, but anything visible from a distance is going to harm the cat's function in the wild or, like the ear thing, would be possible to naturally occur. So it's a common compromise.  Now, this tattoo is usually a very simple tattoo- something along the lines of a blue circle with a line through it, or something to that effect. Simple, noticeable, easily drawn by almost anyone to a level that can be seen and recognized rapidly. But it's a tattoo, and it's on skin. I could certainly see some enterprising newbie tattoo artists getting some volunteer work in tattooing cat (and dog) crotches at a local catch and release program. Sure they're not going to be doing anything fancy, but getting experience with how skin reacts to tattooing, even in that simple quick format, might be helpful!
