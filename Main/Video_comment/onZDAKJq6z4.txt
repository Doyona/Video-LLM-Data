@4:55 Actually, yes, it is the water that does the cutting. Some circumstances (such as cutting bone - yes, they do that, or thinner/softer materials) there really is only the jet and the water. In abrasive water jet, you get certain abrasives (such as Garnet or Aluminum Oxide) that assist in the cutting action. The surfaces are rougher in abrasive water jet, but simple water jet cutting leaves the same cut striations (just less severe). These striations are actually miniature deflections in the high pressure water path as it bores through the material and hits the different crystals or densities within the material. These striations are normal and will be more exaggerated in a more non-homogenous structure (like this foil ball). Also, the jet nozzle is usually the only thing made of a very hard material, such as sapphire or diamond.

The fact is, structural steel (A36 spec, what I beams and H beams are made from) are some of the lowest spec steels available, just going by tensile strength. If you have a vacuum chamber in your shop, here is what you can do with the surface:  1. Get some epoxy with long curing time, and maybe some color you can add to the epoxy.  2. Mix the epoxy and fill half way up in a cylindrical plastic tray with a diameter 50 % larger than your half aluminum ball.  3. Place the half aluminum ball flat face down in the tray on top of the still liquid epoxy and move it to the vacuum chamber.  4. Lower the pressure to a little (not full vacuum).  5. Let the atmosphere back in very slowly so that the epoxy fills all the cavities in the flat surface of the half ball.  6. After the epoxy had hardened, you can sand and polish the surface. You probably have way better procedures for this kind of work from your career, but my point is that if you fill epoxy in the cavities in the surface you will be able to maintain the original structure of the compressed aluminum foil inside the ball after sanding and polishing the flat surface.
