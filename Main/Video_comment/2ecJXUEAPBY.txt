Thet spark plug sparking only over a gap means the spark plug is dirty and it forms a conductive path that carries away the small current from the coil. If you form a gap, there is no leak anymore, the small coil current is sufficient to charge the capacitance of the wire to the high voltage till the spark forms. The spark then acts like a very good switch, discharging the cable capacitance into the spark plug. And because now it is coming from a capacitance, the current is not limited anymore, so it can overcome the dirt leakage, so build enough voltage across the spark gap electrodes to form the spark there too. As far a I know, some spark plugs had an extra spark gap (or a piece of ceramics electrically behaving the same way) inside the ceramic insulator for exactly the same - to help the sparks to form even when electrodes are dirty. But not sure if all spark plugs have that and even when it is there, it uses to be the first thing to fail on the spark plugs that have this extra feature.

Those bulkhead connectors were notorious for having high resistance over time. They would overheat, melt and catch on fire. There would be one heavy gauge wire from the engine compartment through the bulkhead connector, to the ammeter and then back through the bulkhead connector again. In the older models if the ammeter burnt up then the vehicle basically got no power to most circuits. Chrysler charging systems sucked back in the day also. Alernators on most standard duty vehicles were a pathetic 35 amps. The five wire box is the electronic ignition. The pickup coil on these gave the most trouble. The two wire box on the bulkhead (firewall) is the electronic voltage regulator for the alternator. Those usually did not give any trouble.
