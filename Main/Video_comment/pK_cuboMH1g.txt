I'm rarely disappointed in Donut or Zach.  This is one of those times.  When Zach said that drums weren't very good at braking, that is patently false.  For a given diameter, a drum brake is superior to a disk brake for braking.  For instance, a 14 inch drum has nearly twice the braking surface that a 14 inch disk has.  This is because it is using the circumference of the brake, or, the furthest part, so the largest part.  In addition, the brake shoes inside the drum have a larger contact area than the caliper on the outside.  Larger braking surface, and larger pad surface equals better braking.  What drum brakes are very, very bad at is shedding heat.  They also are worse than disk at drying themselves when wet, but it's the heat build up that is the bigger problem.  However, drums do provide better braking than disc always... once or twice...

I love brake booster deletes on these cars too, especially with big brakes, the brake feel is amazing, the travel is WAY less than even with a big booster upgrade, and they are light enough it isnt that much pedal effort once you get used to it. Plus the master cylinder and brake booster have the same bolt spacing, so you can loosen the brake lines, pull forward the stock 13/16" master cylinder, remove the booster, push the master cylinder back against the firewall and run 2 good quality bolts and locking nuts through the firewall to attach the brake booster. You then cut apart the brake booster and remove the adjustable rod with the clevis pin that attaches the brake pedal to the booster, and it fits back right back in between the brake pedal and master cylinder. Tighten the lines, bleed everything, and boom, way better brake feel, super short brake travel, better brake modulation, and brakes that work perfectly even if the engine isnt running, all for the cost of a couple grade 8 bolts and locking nuts. There really is no comparison to the feel of a lightweight car with with manual brakes and a properly sized master cylinder.
