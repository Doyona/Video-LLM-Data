Yeah here's the issues:  1) Compatibility, you need a special bike to use it. When choices are limited, customers tend to look where choices are numerous. Standardization is a great benefit anywhere. Of course with standardization you might get stagnation, so it's not all good.  2) Efficiency. Derailleurs, for all their flaws, are really efficient. They are efficient by their very nature, not just because of a century of development. Gearboxes have good efficiency too thanks to a lot of development, but they will never be as efficient as derailleurs. Close enough maybe, but by the nature of how they operate they won't ever be the most efficient.  3) Cost. Derailleurs can be hella expensive, but you get A LOT with an expensive derailleur. And you get most of those benefits if you don't go for the most expensive options anyways due to standardization and the fact innovations creep down into the cheaper options over time. Gearboxes, due to the limited runs it has, are always expensive. Regardless of how good they are. Even the cheapest gearbox is still overpriced because they aren't made in nearly as much bulk as derailleurs are. Small batches means higher costs, which the customers are expected to pay for. And gearboxes aren't standardized so what you get is what you get, one product might be good at one thing and another is great at another thing. You don't get all the good bits in one gearbox because there's no market standard that drives them to be good at all the things the customers expect them to be.  4) Serviceability. Sure, a gearbox just needs a change of oil every year and they just keep going... But what if they don't? When something breaks you can't just be sure to go to the bike shop to get a small replacement part. The gearbox you bought 3 years ago might not even be supported anymore. You are SOL when that happens, fixing it means getting a new gearbox which might not even be compatible with the rest of your drive line. And that is long before you consider the amount of knowledge and tools you need to start fixing a gearbox as opposed to a derailleur. Yes, derailleurs require more maintenance and cleaning. But they are EASIER to work on when things go wrong.  5) Weight. Not really a big issue when riding on nice trails. But as soon as you need to shift your bike around, any extra pounds will add up. If you have to lift the bike up a rock face, any extra pounds will be felt. And maneuvering a heavy bike, even if the weight is perfectly centered, requires more effort. Effort that would be better spent having fun.  6) Familiarity. With a chain, sprocket and derailleur bike you instinctively know when it's running well and you know ever sound it makes. With a gearbox you don't really know if it's supposed to be that way or sound like it does. So you will not be prepared for when the thing breaks down. I've never been surprised by a derailleur doing strange things and breaking on a ride. I've already noticed the issue long before it does and had my chance to fix it before it broke.  7) Finally, in any other case, for gearboxes to become a viable option they have to become accepted by a not insignificant portion of riders. As long as you only have "nerds" and commuters using them, they will remain a niche product that will never be standardized and will always have a new company making them because "they are so fricking awesome" and then that drive fades away into the history books.  -------------  Mind you, all major derailleur manufacturers actually have done research and development into gearboxes. They have all come to the same conclusion in the end, the gearbox doesn't offer any significant enough benefits to where they are ready to set up a serious manufacturing line for them. Good enough for a small test run, but they just don't stand a chance against the inherent benefits of derailleurs. The times and places a gearbox does shine are so spaced apart and so small that even those that can afford to produce them simply don't. With E-bikes, the trend has of course been leaning more towards gearboxes and other similar developments at the crank. But that's because of necessity where a traditional drive setup would not be enough.  On motorbikes, even a beefed up derailleur wouldn't suffice. It would break way too often.
