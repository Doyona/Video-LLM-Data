Here's the story on the sour notes in American chocolate: Milton Hershey already had a successful line of caramel candies when he toured Europe. It was there that he tried the first inexpensive shelf-stable milk chocolate bar. Milk chocolate existed before then but was extremely expensive. After one bite, he knew that milk chocolate was the future of candy. So when he got back to the US, he sold his caramel candy factory and founded a new company town (Hershey PA). He started building factories to make milk chocolate in the US right away. This was a little crazy since he didn't actually know how to mass produce milk chocolate yet. He hired a chemist who told him that you can't just add milk to chocolate to make shelf-stable milk chocolate. In Europe they used powdered milk but Hershey decided to go with sweetened condensed milk instead -- that might be because liquid ingredients are easier to work with when you are mass producing food. Anyway, the chemist couldn't figure out how to mass produce sweetened condensed milk w/o burning it. So he let a one of his workers have a go. The worker boiled the milk in a vat that was under a partial vacuum. The vacuum allowed the milk to condense w/o burning but there was also some fermentation that produced butyric acid. Butyric acid is present in some cheeses but also vomit. Human taste is a little strange in that we often equate a flavor with our first experience having it. If the first time you tried butyric acid is when you were sick then you might remember that the next time you try it. Anyway, the worker offered to try to remove the butyric acid but Hershey seemed to like the sour note that it gave the chocolate. Since Americans are used to a sour note in our chocolate, if Hershey were to take it out then it would taste like something is missing. If the European chocolate had an ingredient that was an acquired taste then no doubt Americans would be stupid and unsophisticated for not liking it. But since it is in American chocolate, Americans are considered stupid for liking an ingredient in our chocolate that is also found in some cheeses.
