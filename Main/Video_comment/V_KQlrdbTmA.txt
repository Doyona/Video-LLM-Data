Put two CVTs (those ATV ones with a belt) in this thing - one per track -  and use forks on the pulleys which adjust the ratios. This way you don't lose power if you steer, you actually gain power â€“ as the slowing track's energy is recouped and the engine power goes still fully to both tracks.  Then connect the ratio changing forks to a steering wheel. So center is 50% and 50% of the ratio and completely to one side disengages the engine on the side, by pulling the pully fully out.  Then install a split brake pedal, so you can slow the disengages track down, if it's "rolling".  Then throw out the gearbox and replace it with a CVT as well, plus a rpm governor on the engine. So you set it to a given power output and the CVT adjusts the ratio, depending on the load you throw at it.  You can then put a maximum speed pedal on the floor or a speed lever somewhere and this would limit the travel of the CVT. So if there's tough terrain the CVT would adjust down, to not overload the engine, but if the terrain gets easy the speed pedal would limit how fast the vehicle can go.  If you think one CVT may be a bit overpowered by the engine, just put two side by side for the "gearbox" part, as the steering CVTs will also share the load evenly.   In the end, you'll just need a real gearbox to add a backwards gear. So no clutch required, as the CVT will act as a clutch here.  This should be able to shave off some weight and eliminate most of the cooling needs: No band brakes, no hot gearbox.

You need a Maybach double differential with a hydrostatic steering input.  Caterpillar did this for the old Cat Challenger tractors and the swash plate in the hy-stat was connected to a traditional steering wheel.  The result is track drive with differential steering, but you just drive it like a car.  Only drawback was that the turn radius was tied to the forward speed.  Going fast, turning the steering wheel all the way to the lock resulted in a wide turn radius, but at slower speeds it resulted in a tighter turning radius.  If you were stopped and turned the steering wheel, it would turn in place.  (with rubber tracks, this resulted in a lot of hopping and shaking.)  On snow, you would probably just dig rather than turning in place.  It takes a little getting used to, but once you do it is great.  Turn the steering wheel a little, and one track slows down a little and the other speeds up a little.

I'm sure you folks know, but for those who don't, the metal that makes up the sides of that cage thing on the back of the Bombi is what's called "Expanded Metal" and it's made from steel. There's 2 versions that I know of: Raised, and flattened. The expanded metal on the cage is raised, which typically provides more traction / grip, but does have a lot of sharp edges to it. (And it's not meant to be used for the sides of a cage. It's usually used on things you walk on that get wet, like trailers, outdoor stairs, etc.) And with it being steel, it's for sure heavy. Flattened metal, is just raised metal that's been fed through rollers that flatten it, which removes a majority of the sharp edges, makes the thickness more uniform and easier to work with, etc, while retaining the strength of the raised.  Making the floor out of aluminum like Matt says he wants to do makes sense to me. Aluminum is lighter and typically just as strong as steel at the cost of being more expensive. If you're trying to stay on top of something that can give way (like snow, sand, mud crust, etc) then making your vehicle as light as possible will certainly help. Should the fuel pump wear out, the engine will fail to start when left to sit overnight. This is due to the minimum pressure required for the injectors.  12v won't generate the needed pressure, yet 14.5v will (jump start). The engine will also start from residual pressure, but will leak down after an extended period.  Aftermarket fuel system, IMO.
