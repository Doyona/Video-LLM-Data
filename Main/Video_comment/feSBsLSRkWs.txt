Increasing methane emissions are a major contributor to the rising concentration of greenhouse gases in Earth's atmosphere, and are responsible for up to one-third of near-term global heating.[1][2] During 2019, about 60% (360 million tons) of methane released globally was from human activities, while natural sources contributed about 40% (230 million tons).[3][4] Reducing methane emissions by capturing and utilizing the gas can produce simultaneous environmental and economic benefits.[1][5] About one-third (33%) of anthropogenic emissions are from gas release during the extraction and delivery of fossil fuels; mostly due to gas venting and gas leaks. Animal agriculture is a similarly large source (30%); primarily because of enteric fermentation by ruminant livestock such as cattle and sheep. Human consumer waste flows, especially those passing through landfills and wastewater treatment, have grown to become a third major category (18%). Plant agriculture, including both food and biomass production, constitutes a fourth group (15%), with rice production being the largest single contributor.[1][6] The world's wetlands contribute about three-quarters (75%) of the enduring natural sources of methane.[3][4] Seepages from near-surface hydrocarbon and clathrate hydrate deposits, volcanic releases, wildfires, and termite emissions account for much of the remainder.[6] Contributions from the surviving wild populations of ruminant mammals are vastly overwhelmed by those of cattle, humans, and other livestock animals.[7]

Whilst i agree very much that ethically and locally sourced beef is not as bad as it could be, please do note that methane is NOT directly comparable with Carbon Dioxide (CO2) when it comes to its potential for global warming, ie the absorption of solar energy.  Global Warming Potential (GWP) was developed as a metric for precisely this reason, and on this scale, which considers both absorptive capability of the gas, but also its mean residency in the atmosphere.  The GWP scale has CO2 as its reference gas, so CO2 has a GWP of 1. Methane (CH4) is currently considered to have a GWP of between 28 to 36. ie 1 kg of methane causes the same global warming as between 28 to 36 kg of CO2.   This is a signifcant problem, and one of the reasons that methane is coming under such close scrutiny!

The other thing to understand is how the 'greenhouse' effect works.  Sunlight is a range of frequencies from the electromagnetic spectrum.  All matter has the ability to absorb energy from the electromagnetic spectrum but different materials absorb different frequencies.  This is how radio works - metals absorb energy at a specific frequency.  Water absorbs energy at another frequency - microwaves.  Carbon dioxide and methane absorb energy at other specific frequencies.  Solar panels absorb energy at specific frequencies.  Plants photosynthesis by absorbing energy from specific frequencies.  But the energy in sunlight is not infinite.  There is an finite amount of energy and it is distributed amongst the different frequencies of sunlight.  Once all the energy at a specific frequency has been captured by a material then there is no more to capture so it doesn't matter if you increase the amount of matter.  The upshot is once the level of CO2 goes above a certain amount it will have absorbed all the energy at that specific frequency and increasing the amount of CO2 will have no further effect.  The people who make solar panels have known this since the 1960s when two engineers, Shockley and Queisser, published a paper on the subject.  To get greater than about 30% efficiency they have to make panels with multiple layers where each layer captures energy from a different frequency.  It's all known.
