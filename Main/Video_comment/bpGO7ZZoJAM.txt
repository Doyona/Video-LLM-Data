As for meals, at 3 meals a day eating only conbini food, that's another 15 on food, so another 210. So on basic bare minimum needs, that's about 420. If you are fine with walking to most places , that's about 20 a day for public transportation (which is a lot for it, as you likely wouldn't be going across town 3 or 4 times the same day), so it's about 250. You can go cheaper if you simply walk more, but that's unlikely to happen. This also takes into account train rides to different provinces. That leaves you a total of 330 for fun, out of a tiny 1k USD budget. Honestly, you'd probably want to save more time, with better board, and real food, so I recommend $2k. But it's totally doable for even less than 1k, if you are there more for the experience than the material goods or fancy foods.  I think even 500 is doable for two weeks (as some Japanese people themselves live on 500 USD a month), but you'd basically walk everywhere, live in the few existing hostels left or the cheapest manga cafes, and  starve with only one conbini meal a day. Great guide. Couple of things to add that may be of use to people.  At least one side of the Tokyo Metropolitan Government building is open most of the time, but both are closed on the first and third Tuesday of every month. Many other places have similarly odd schedules. As you may be in different cities, or even different areas within big cities like Tokyo or Osaka only for a short time, be sure to check the opening times in advance as much as possible.  Eating cheaply is surprisingly easy, however while it's nice to be spontaneous, it can be difficult to judge a place from the outside, and sometimes it may not be obvious how expensive it is. Some fantastic places are also totally invisible from the street (on high floors etc). Much as you would at home, you can easily book a sit down lunch or dinner in advance over the internet at many places, but crucially you can often choose (and pay in advance for) a set menu when you book. This avoids a lot of awkwardness, any surprise costs, and largely negates the language barrier as you do not even have to order. I wouldn't suggest trying to book every meal, not least because it's fun to try out your Japanese, but if you are on a set budget and want to guarantee you eat well for a price you can afford, this is a great option.  Yes it is cheap to go to baths and even many onsen, but be aware that tattoos are still taboo in Japan due to their association with organised crime. You will be asked to cover any tattoos in many places, and if you have many you may not be admitted at all. If you are heavily tattooed it might be a good idea to skip any public bathing, or check in advance with the place if possible.   Finally, yes the JR pass is now a rip off. Suica cards are basically impossible to get hold of, but absolutely are the best way to travel. The tourist versions are also not very available right now, and are only available from specific places when they are (like you could not get one at Narita last time I checked), so do your research in advance and be prepared to pay cash for tickets at least for a while. IIRC you also cannot charge a Suica card using a credit card, cash only, so be ready for that. If you have an iPhone you can use the virtual card, however, the vast majority of Android phones (at least from outside of Japan) do not have the necessary hardware to work with their turnstiles, and will not work!

As a frequently backpacker to Japan, I find supermarket ready to eat food extremely underrated. I could get a bowl on radish soup and vegetables for Y350 and some rice and ginger pork for another Y500. A very balanced, warm and local meal. I usually get some daifuku for dessert, around Y200 each. My suggestion to travelers willing to spend a bit more on food is to go to food halls (usually in the basement) in shopping malls after 7pm. Really good food at a very cheap price and the variety is unmatched. As for transport, JR regional pass and city day pass is the way to go. I recently backpacked Kyushu, inspired by your Journey across japan kyushu videos. I went to Fukuoka, Kumamoto, Kagoshima, Nagasaki prefectures in about a week and spent around Y30,000 on transport and exclusively taking the bullet train between the cities. My daily transport within the city was capped at Y300 because I bought the day pass. For travelers on a budget, the best advice I can give is to avoid the golden route at all cost. Meaning Tokyo, Osaka, Kyoto and Nara. There's so much more that Japan has to offer outside of the golden route, at a fraction of the cost and hardly any tourist. If you're looking for an itinerary, Abroad in Japan is undoubtedly one of the best sources.
