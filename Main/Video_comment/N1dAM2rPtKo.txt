There are regulations with land usage and you can't just build a tower where a house used to be. Every plot of land is associated with two ratios :   Land Occupancy Ratio: This refers to the percentage of the land that can be built upon in relation to the total land area. In Tokyo and other cities facing space constraints, regulations govern how much of a plot can be occupied by buildings. For instance, if the land occupancy ratio is 60%, it means that only 60% of the land can be covered by structures, while the remaining 40% must be left as open space, including setbacks, gardens, and driveways.  Floor Area Ratio (FAR) or Floor Space Index (FSI): This ratio represents the total floor area of the building(s) relative to the size of the land. For example, if the FAR is 300%, it means that the total floor area of the building(s) can be up to three times the size of the land. This can allow for taller buildings or more extensive structures on relatively smaller plots of land, allowing for more efficient use of space, especially in densely populated urban areas like Tokyo. Given you have such a broad audience, I would heavily caution you against spreading missinformation regarding the effect of zoning controls on housing prices.   Here in South-East Queensland in Australia (comprising several urban localities including Brisbane) over the past decade we have experienced substantial uplift in zoning capacity in centres and regional areas. Contrary to this, local research shows that there was no decernable impacts from this uplift in zoning on housing prices.
