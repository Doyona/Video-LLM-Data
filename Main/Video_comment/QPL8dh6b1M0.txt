The main cause of the failure was due to poor Engineering management, engineering execution, lack of coordination between Engineering, Construction and M&R departments. The M&R department is responsible for the district regulator stations. They must be involved in any work done within 50-100 feet of a regulator station. This was not the case at Columbia gas the M&R department was not involved in the new main installation at the district regulator. There was also a problem with drawings for the regulator station the locations of the sense lines were not included on PID drawings so Engineering did not consider this when they were connecting new main to the low pressure system. The M&R department should also be involved whenever new main is installed to a regulator station.  Besides the requirement for a PE stamp on engineering drawings the state of MASS implemented some additional controls to low pressure regulator stations. This included adding a fail safe shut off valve upstream of the monitor regulator. The shut off valve has a dual acting pilot that will close the regulator when the downstream pressure goes above 16 inches or drops below 6 inches. This is what happened at the regulator station in Lawrence because the sense lines connected to the old cast iron main lost pressure which as you explained caused the worker regulator to go wide open introducing high pressure into the low pressure system. There is another potential common mode failure that you did not mention. The regulator uses the high pressure upstream to close the sleeve on the regulator in a normally open regulator. There are regulators that use the high pressure to compress a spring that opens the regulator valve. This is a normally closed regulator. The power gas for the regulators is generally tapped off the upstream high pressure pipe at the regulator but not always sometimes it comes from the high pressure main. If you lose the power gas then a normally open regulator will not close. If both the monitor and worker are normally open regulator that use higher pressure to close the regulator then they will both be open. The new design for low pressure regulator station uses a normally open worker and a normally closed monitor and a slam shut with a dual acting pilot. There is still a potential failure that while not catastrophic is equally dangerous if the regulator and/or slam shut closes on a single feed system. In this scenario the gas pressure drops and the customers equipment will shut off. When this happens all meters connected to the system need to be shut off individually before the system can be repressurized.  Depending on the number of customers this can take a few days to weeks. Interesting video on the subject but I thought I would share some additional information about the changes the state made after the disaster. The addition of the PE stamp on engineering drawings is a plus but the reengineering of the design of the regulator stations is more critical in preventing this from happening in the future. Older cities in the Northeast have thousands of these low pressure regulator stations and it will take a long time and a lot of money to upgrade them to a safer design. The idea of moving low pressure distribution systems to high pressure distribution systems is good but there are thousands if not millions of old low pressure pipes that would need to be installed that is also expensive and time consuming. Engineering is always a game of trade offs.

Hey, I just got a job recently as a compliance engineer with a natural gas utility! I got to learn all about this because it directly informs some of our operations and maintenance procedures.  Low pressure (LP) systems are a headache, and old cast iron mains more generally. Our biggest capital project right now is replacing all of them with steel or polyethylene. Typically in our system (and I assume it's common elsewhere), the distribution lines right up to the house are medium pressure (~25 psig) and the regulator is on the meter set which brings it down to ~1/4 psig.  Also because of this event we now install excess flow valves (EFVs) on *all* lines we repair or install. EFVs trip like breakers if the flow through them exceeds a certain value, so if a line with an EFV is compromised it effectively shuts itself off at the main (it still leaks slowly, but we can come fix it before it becomes a real problem).  Also it wasn't mentioned specifically (and may be obvious), but the reason the second regulator is set to a slightly higher pressure than the first is so that you know if the primary regulator fails - it shows up on SCADA as an abnormal (but, importantly, still safe) pressure downstream. Ours are set to 30 psig, I believe, instead of the typical 25. Thanks for the video Grady!
Minor point: Given that many pipelines run "high" pressures in the 300-450 psig range, it would make more sense to refer to "not-low" pressures with at-house regulators as "medium" pressure systems. This would better match up with how "bottled" gas is often described as well.

Aside from the main error which caused the accident, this sounds like another example of people not understanding basic probability - that redundant systems (in this case 2 pressure sensors, monitor and worker) are only redundant if they operate independently. If they're both vulnerable to the same failure mode, then they're no longer independent and thus no longer redundant.  Other examples include:  - The Space Shuttle Challenger, where they were having trouble with exhaust gases in the solid rocket boosters escaping past O-ring seals, so they added a third O-ring. Completely overlooking that factors which caused two O-rings to fail (like low temperature) could also cause the third O-ring to fail.  - The Fukushima Daiichi nuclear plant, where they had something like a dozen backup generators in case the plant ever lost electrical power for its cooling pumps. Only 5-7 generators were needed to provide sufficient power to cool the plant, and the rest were for redundancy. But they placed all the generators and diesel fuel in the same basement thereby voiding that redundancy. So when the tsunami flooded the basement it wiped out all their generators and fuel simultaneously, precipitating the disaster. Putting half the generators and fuel at a different location at a different altitude could have prevented the whole thing.  - Backing up your computer data on an external drive, then storing it in a drawer right next to your computer. If your house burns down, you will lose both the backup and the original data on the computer. After you backup your data, you should store the backup drive somewhere else. Like in your drawer at work. (This is the main reason I now advocate cloud backups, even though there are still legitimate privacy concerns. It's less hassle than shuffling a backup drive between two locations.)

The second regulator, which under normal circumstances would never activate, should have been of a different, simple design, with local sensing. Better yet, it should have been an overpressure shutdown valve. What if the first regulator fails, the second regulator takes over, but nobody ever bothers to check them? You could be running on the second regulator, with zero redundancy, for months or years without ever knowing it. Redundancy without monitoring is false redundancy.  Even on the first regulator, with the remote sensing, there should have been a sense line directly after the valve, that can take over the regulation if the pressure there becomes to high. In (lab) power supplies with remote voltage sensing, it's common to connect the local output voltage to the sense line with a diode. That way, if the sense line is ever disconnected, the output voltage can only rise by the forward voltage drop of the diode (about 0.7V).  In case of the gas regulators, then sense lines could have been blocked, severed, or otherwise inoperable, and there should still have been a local backup sensing, with a slightly higher pressure setting.
