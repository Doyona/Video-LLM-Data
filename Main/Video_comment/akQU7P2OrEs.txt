Just as an aside, when parrying with a sword you try as much as possible to avoid parrying with the edge, generally you want to either parry with the flat if it is a double edged blade, or to parry with the back if it is single edged. Just because the edge is not a set of spinning teeth does NOT mean the swords blade cannot be damaged in a badly timed parry. Another aspect of parrying with a sword (preferably you use your shield for parrying rather than the sword, or move out of the way) is that you angle to allow the opponents weapon to slide off the blade, so spreading out the impact over a more extended period, thus reducing the risk of damage to the blade.   This is *especially* important with certain swords, the Japanese Katana being an excellent example. The hard outer layer of steel holds an exceptional edge but on the flip side it is also far more prone to parrying damage than more homogenous Western swords. A badly timed parry with a Katana can easily badly damage the cutting edge, or even break the blade entirely.
