I can't find one detail that is quite important here. Are the pins bridged also on the GPU itself or is the bridge in the adapter the only one? If they are bridged on the GPU, then the flimsy bridge in the adapter should not cause heating up. Electricity will find the path of least resistance and would simply go through the middle 4 pins, then spread to all 6 lanes. If it's the only bridge, then it might be possible that the card tries to pull more power from the outside pins and the only path for it to go is through the tiny bridge. The problem here is that nobody reproduced this behavior, slight increase in temperatures might be just a result of using 5 or 4 out of 6 cables and joints, not enough to cause a meltdown. Besides that, if that was the point that generates too much heat, why would only the outside pins melt? It's a bridge between outside pins and the second from the outside. It would actually make more sense that the outside pins have better cooling than the ones inside and it would melt from inside out. In all cases it's the outside pin that gets hot enough to melt the plastic, so the heat source should not really be in between two pins and only cause one to melt. Even if we'd consider that the outside pin has lower thermal mass (cable ripped off) than the second from outside (cable still connected and bridged to the 3rd one), it would look quite unusual. What seems to be much more likely cause is that the cable is only slightly ripped off. Enough to still let the current through but with much higher resistance causing  much more heat. This way the heat source would be only on the outside pin and the second from the outside would get less of this heat transferred through the bridge. This would also match everything we know so far. It's caused by bending it and cracking the solder or the whole pad, the cable is still connected but the connection is very small and heats up. In this case it would not matter if it's bridged or not but rather if the cable can partially rip off the pin pad. The way to test it would be to cut only a bit of the pad of the outside pins and see if they heat up under load. Probably we would see some spectacular meltdown. Even more, it might be reproducible only on setups when the outside pins are connected to separate rails in the power supply. After all, if it was just one rail split into two wires and one of those wires has higher resistance because of the cut, most of the current would divert through the one that is in tact. If they are separate rails (not bridged anywhere else between PSU and the adapter), it is possible that it would try to pull fairly high current through the damaged pad as there is no other path for it to go and it is still connected. Instead of this sort of testing, I would just go on with manual test by putting a load on each pin lines separately and testing how it behaves, what's the temperature and how bending it affects the results. Most likely under high load on the outside pin and the outside wire you will see resistance spike when you bend it enough.  What's important is that it's not enough to have this bottleneck that will heat up under high current but also have a situation in which the current would actually flow there. If there is a bridge on GPU side, it will divert to use pins with less resistance. If there is a bridge on PSU side, it will divert to use pin pads with less resistance. Only if there are no bridges on both sides and the bridge in the adapter has fairly high resistance, it might actually pull the power straight through the broken pad that has much higher resistance. Simply because there's no better way for it to flow.
